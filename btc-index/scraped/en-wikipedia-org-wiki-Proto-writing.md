**Proto-writing** consists of visible marks [communicating](/wiki/Communication "Communication") limited information.[2] Such systems emerged from earlier traditions of [symbol](/wiki/Symbol "Symbol") systems in the early [Neolithic](/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic"), as early as the 7th millennium BC in [China](/wiki/History_of_China "History of China") and [southeastern Europe](/wiki/History_of_the_Balkans "History of the Balkans"). Some of the earliest known evidence of proto-writing dates to over 40,000 years ago.[3] They used [ideographic](/wiki/Ideographic "Ideographic") or early [mnemonic](/wiki/Mnemonic "Mnemonic") symbols or both to represent a limited number of concepts, in contrast to true [writing systems](/wiki/Writing_systems "Writing systems"), which record the [language](/wiki/Language "Language") of the writer.[4]

## Paleolithic

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Proto-writing&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Paleolithic")]

In 2022, a team led by amateur archaeologist [Bennett Bacon](/w/index.php?title=Bennett_Bacon&action=edit&redlink=1 "Bennett Bacon \(page does not exist\)")[5] presented an analysis of lines, dots and "Y"-like symbols on [Upper Palaeolithic](/wiki/Upper_Palaeolithic "Upper Palaeolithic") cave paintings as indicating the mating cycle of animals in a [lunar calendar](/wiki/Lunar_calendar "Lunar calendar"). The markings found in over 400 caves across Europe were compared to the mating cycles of the animals with which they were associated, showing a correlation with the month of the year in which the animals depicted in the cave paintings would typically give birth. The markings were 20,000 years old, predating attested proto-writing systems by 10,000 years.[6][7] In 2026, researchers at [Saarland University](/wiki/Saarland_University "Saarland University") published a study with evidence of proto-writing from over 40,000 years ago in central Europe.[8][3]

## Neolithic

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Proto-writing&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Neolithic")]

[](/wiki/File:%E6%B2%B3%E5%8D%97%E8%88%9E%E9%98%B3%E8%B4%BE%E6%B9%96%E9%81%97%E5%9D%80-%E5%88%BB%E7%AC%A6%E9%BE%9F%E7%94%B2-%E8%A3%B4%E6%9D%8E%E5%B2%97%E6%96%87%E5%8C%96-%E6%B2%B3%E5%8D%97%E5%8D%9A%E7%89%A9%E9%99%A2.jpg)Turtle plastron from Jiahu inscribed with an eye-like symbol[9][10]

### Neolithic China

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Proto-writing&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Neolithic China")]

Further information: [Neolithic symbols in China](/wiki/Neolithic_symbols_in_China "Neolithic symbols in China")

In 2003, turtle shells with [carved inscriptions featuring a library of symbols](/wiki/Jiahu_symbols "Jiahu symbols") were found in 24 Neolithic graves excavated at [Jiahu](/wiki/Jiahu "Jiahu") in the northern Chinese province of [Henan](/wiki/Henan "Henan"). Using [radiocarbon dating](/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating "Radiocarbon dating"), the inscriptions have been dated to the 7th millennium BC. According to some archaeologists, the symbols bear a resemblance to the first attested [oracle bone inscriptions](/wiki/Oracle_bone_inscriptions "Oracle bone inscriptions") dating to c. 1200 BC.[11][_[unreliable source?](/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources "Wikipedia:Reliable sources")_] Others have dismissed this claim as insufficiently substantiated, claiming that simple geometric designs such as those found on the Jiahu shells cannot be linked to early writing.[12]

### Neolithic Southeastern Europe

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Proto-writing&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Neolithic Southeastern Europe")]

[](/wiki/File:Tartaria_amulet.png)Clay amulet, one of the [Tărtăria tablets](/wiki/T%C4%83rt%C4%83ria_tablets "Tărtăria tablets"), dated to c. 5300 BC

The [Vinča symbols](/wiki/Vin%C4%8Da_symbols "Vinča symbols") (6th–5th millennia BC) are an evolution of simple undeciphered symbols first attested during the 7th millennium BC from [Vinča culture](/wiki/Vin%C4%8Da_culture "Vinča culture"). Over time, the symbols gradually became more complex, ultimately culminating in the [Tărtăria tablets](/wiki/T%C4%83rt%C4%83ria_tablets "Tărtăria tablets") (c. 5300 BC).[13] The symbols went out of use around 3500 BC.[14]

They have sometimes been described as an example of proto-writing,[15] with most scholars agreeing that the symbols indicate ownership or other information, but do not record any language.[16]

Another example of proto-writing might be recorded on the [Dispilio Tablet](/wiki/Dispilio_Tablet "Dispilio Tablet") in Greece (6th millennium BC), however, as of 2025, there has not been a proper academic publication on that artifact yet.[17]

## Chalcolithic and Early Bronze Age

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Proto-writing&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Chalcolithic and Early Bronze Age")]

During c. 3600 – c. 3200 BC, proto-writing in the [Fertile Crescent](/wiki/Fertile_Crescent "Fertile Crescent") was gradually evolving into [cuneiform](/wiki/Cuneiform "Cuneiform"), the earliest mature writing system. 

### Mesopotamia

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Proto-writing&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Mesopotamia")]

Further information: [Proto-cuneiform](/wiki/Proto-cuneiform "Proto-cuneiform"), [Cuneiform § History](/wiki/Cuneiform#History "Cuneiform"), and [History of Mesopotamia](/wiki/History_of_Mesopotamia "History of Mesopotamia")

According to some scholars, the [proto-cuneiform](/wiki/Proto-cuneiform "Proto-cuneiform") tablets (c. 3350–3000 BC) reflects the stage of writing, when what would become the cuneiform script of [Sumer](/wiki/Sumer "Sumer") was still in the proto-writing stage, because it was a system based on numerical and logographic signs, lacking phonetic signs (or only a few). This system was not intended to record a language. The transitional stage to a proper writing system takes place during the first centuries of the [Early Dynastic Period](/wiki/Early_Dynastic_Period_\(Mesopotamia\) "Early Dynastic Period \(Mesopotamia\)") (c. 2900–2700 BC).[18]

### Egypt

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Proto-writing&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Egypt")]

Further information: [Egyptian hieroglyphs § History and evolution](/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs#History_and_evolution "Egyptian hieroglyphs"), and [Prehistoric Egypt](/wiki/Prehistoric_Egypt "Prehistoric Egypt")

A similar development took place in the genesis of the [Egyptian hieroglyphs](/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs "Egyptian hieroglyphs"). Various scholars believe that Egyptian hieroglyphs "came into existence a little after [Sumerian script](/wiki/Sumerian_script "Sumerian script"), and ... probably [were] ... invented under the influence of the latter ...",[19] although it is pointed out and held that "the evidence for such direct influence remains flimsy" and that "a very credible argument can also be made for the independent development of writing in Egypt ..."[20]

## Bronze Age

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Proto-writing&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Bronze Age")]

During the [Bronze Age](/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age"), the cultures of the [Ancient Near East](/wiki/Ancient_Near_East "Ancient Near East") are known to have had fully developed writing systems, while the marginal territories affected by the Bronze Age, such as Europe, India and China, remained in the stage of proto-writing.[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The [Chinese script](/wiki/Chinese_script "Chinese script") emerged from proto-writing in the [Chinese Bronze Age](/wiki/Chinese_Bronze_Age "Chinese Bronze Age"), during about the 14th to 11th centuries BC ([Oracle bone script](/wiki/Oracle_bone_script "Oracle bone script")), while symbol systems native to Europe and India are extinct and were replaced by descendants of the [Semitic abjad](/wiki/Semitic_abjad "Semitic abjad") during the [Iron Age](/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age").[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[](/wiki/File:Indus_seal_impression.jpg)Typical "Indus script" seal impression showing an "inscription" of five characters

### Indian Bronze Age

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Proto-writing&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Indian Bronze Age")]

Main article: [Indus script](/wiki/Indus_script "Indus script")

The [Indus script](/wiki/Indus_script "Indus script") is a symbol system that emerged during the end of the 4th millennium BC in the [Indus Valley Civilisation](/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilisation "Indus Valley Civilisation"). 

### European Bronze Age

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Proto-writing&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: European Bronze Age")]

With the exception of the [Aegean](/wiki/Aegean_civilization "Aegean civilization") and mainland Greece ([Linear A](/wiki/Linear_A "Linear A"), [Linear B](/wiki/Linear_B "Linear B"), [Cretan hieroglyphs](/wiki/Cretan_hieroglyphs "Cretan hieroglyphs")), the early writing systems of the Near East did not reach [Bronze Age Europe](/wiki/Bronze_Age_Europe "Bronze Age Europe"). The earliest writing systems of Europe arise in the [Iron Age](/wiki/European_Iron_Age "European Iron Age"), derived from the [Phoenician alphabet](/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet "Phoenician alphabet"). 

However, there are number of interpretations regarding symbols found on artefacts of the European Bronze Age which amount to interpreting them as an indigenous tradition of proto-writing. Of special interest in this context are the [Central European](/wiki/Central_Europe "Central Europe") Bronze Age cultures derived from the [Beaker culture](/wiki/Beaker_culture "Beaker culture") in the second half of the 2nd millennium BC. Interpretations of the markings of the bronze [sickles](/wiki/Sickle "Sickle") associated with the [Urnfield culture](/wiki/Urnfield_culture "Urnfield culture"), especially the large number of so-called "knob-sickles" discovered in the [Frankleben hoard](/wiki/Frankleben_hoard "Frankleben hoard"), are discussed by Sommerfeld (1994).[21] Sommerfeld favours an interpretation of these symbols as [numerals](/wiki/Prehistoric_numerals "Prehistoric numerals") associated with a [lunar calendar](/wiki/Lunar_calendar "Lunar calendar").[22][_[full citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include "Wikipedia:Citing sources")_]

## Later proto-writing

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Proto-writing&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Later proto-writing")]

Even after the Bronze Age, several cultures went through a period of using systems of proto-writing as an intermediate stage before the adoption of writing proper. The "[Slavic runes](/wiki/Pre-Cyrillic_Slavic_writing "Pre-Cyrillic Slavic writing")" (7th/8th century) mentioned by a few medieval authors may have been such a system. Another example is the system of pictographs invented by [Uyaquk](/wiki/Uyaquk "Uyaquk") before the development of the [Yugtun syllabary](/wiki/Yugtun_script "Yugtun script") (c. 1900).[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
