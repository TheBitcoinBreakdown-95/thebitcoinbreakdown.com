**John Forbes Nash Jr.** (June 13, 1928 – May 23, 2015), known and published as **John Nash** , was an American mathematician who made fundamental contributions to [game theory](/wiki/Game_theory "Game theory"), [real algebraic geometry](/wiki/Real_algebraic_geometry "Real algebraic geometry"), [differential geometry](/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), and [partial differential equations](/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation").[1][2] Nash and fellow game theorists [John Harsanyi](/wiki/John_Harsanyi "John Harsanyi") and [Reinhard Selten](/wiki/Reinhard_Selten "Reinhard Selten") were awarded the 1994 [Nobel Prize in Economics](/wiki/Nobel_Memorial_Prize_in_Economic_Sciences "Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences").[3] In 2015, [Louis Nirenberg](/wiki/Louis_Nirenberg "Louis Nirenberg") and he were awarded the [Abel Prize](/wiki/Abel_Prize "Abel Prize") for their contributions to the field of partial differential equations. 

As a graduate student in the [Princeton University Department of Mathematics](/wiki/Princeton_University_Department_of_Mathematics "Princeton University Department of Mathematics"), Nash introduced a number of concepts (including the [Nash equilibrium](/wiki/Nash_equilibrium "Nash equilibrium") and the [Nash bargaining solution](/wiki/Nash_bargaining_solution "Nash bargaining solution")), which are now considered central to game theory and its applications in various sciences. In the 1950s, Nash discovered and proved the [Nash embedding theorems](/wiki/Nash_embedding_theorems "Nash embedding theorems") by solving a system of nonlinear partial differential equations arising in [Riemannian geometry](/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry"). This work, also introducing a preliminary form of the [Nash–Moser theorem](/wiki/Nash%E2%80%93Moser_theorem "Nash–Moser theorem"), was later recognized by the [American Mathematical Society](/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society "American Mathematical Society") with the [Leroy P. Steele Prize for Seminal Contribution to Research](/wiki/Leroy_P._Steele_Prize_for_Seminal_Contribution_to_Research "Leroy P. Steele Prize for Seminal Contribution to Research"). [Ennio De Giorgi](/wiki/Ennio_De_Giorgi "Ennio De Giorgi") and Nash found, with separate methods, a body of results paving the way for a systematic understanding of [elliptic](/wiki/Elliptic_partial_differential_equation "Elliptic partial differential equation") and [parabolic partial differential equations](/wiki/Parabolic_partial_differential_equation "Parabolic partial differential equation"). Their De Giorgi–Nash theorem on the smoothness of solutions of such equations resolved [Hilbert's nineteenth problem](/wiki/Hilbert%27s_nineteenth_problem "Hilbert's nineteenth problem") on regularity in the [calculus of variations](/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations"), which had been a well-known [open problem](/wiki/Open_problem "Open problem") for almost 60 years. 

In 1959, Nash began showing signs of mental illness and spent several years at [psychiatric hospitals](/wiki/Psychiatric_hospital "Psychiatric hospital") being treated for [schizophrenia](/wiki/Schizophrenia "Schizophrenia"). After 1970, his condition slowly improved, allowing him to return to academic work by the mid-1980s.[4]

Nash's life was the subject of [Sylvia Nasar](/wiki/Sylvia_Nasar "Sylvia Nasar")'s 1998 biographical book _[A Beautiful Mind](/wiki/A_Beautiful_Mind_\(book\) "A Beautiful Mind \(book\)")_ , and his struggles with his illness and his recovery became the basis for a 2001 [film of the same name](/wiki/A_Beautiful_Mind_\(film\) "A Beautiful Mind \(film\)") directed by [Ron Howard](/wiki/Ron_Howard "Ron Howard"), in which Nash was portrayed by [Russell Crowe](/wiki/Russell_Crowe "Russell Crowe").[5][6][7][8]
