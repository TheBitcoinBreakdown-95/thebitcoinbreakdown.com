## Part 2: Source Inventory

### Bitcoin's National Security Benefits for the U.S. (BTC Policy / Matthew Pines)

1. Global, organic demand for Bitcoin and USD-based stablecoins may help counter China's geopolitical ambitions for the digital yuan and the Belt and Road Initiative. (BTC Policy / Matthew Pines)
2. If Bitcoin were to monetize to reach close parity with gold, the U.S. (holding the most Bitcoin) would gain relative to adversaries -- serving as an asymmetric hedge against tail risks in the Treasury market. (BTC Policy / Matthew Pines)
3. A gradual shift to a global monetary system centered on a neutral reserve asset that the U.S. holds a large share of would allow the U.S. to reverse structural trade flows that have eroded manufacturing capacity and made it vulnerable to Chinese supply chains. (BTC Policy / Matthew Pines)
4. As the foundation for an emerging "internet of value," Bitcoin (and second layers like Lightning Network) may drive domestic innovation and transform the U.S. digital economy. (BTC Policy / Matthew Pines)
5. Bitcoin mining (which the U.S. leads) may generate positive externalities for the energy system -- as a buyer of first and last resort for renewables and as a flexible load grid resource. (BTC Policy / Matthew Pines)
6. "Can the U.S. harness emerging monetary networks like Bitcoin to counter China's digital authoritarianism and bolster its geopolitical advantage?" -- BTC Policy framing. (BTC Policy / Matthew Pines)

### De-Dollarization Threat Context (Financial Times)

7. The dollar-based monetary order is being challenged by the spread of de-dollarization efforts and central bank digital currencies (CBDCs). (FT)
8. China and India have been paying for Russian commodities in renminbi, rupees and UAE dirhams -- de-dollarization of trade flows may proliferate with BRICS expansion. (FT)
9. An emerging CBDC-based network enforced with bilateral currency swap lines could enable central banks in the global east and south to intermediate currency flows without referencing the dollar or touching the western banking system. (FT)
10. Current account surpluses of China, Russia and Saudi Arabia are at a record, but are largely not being recycled into traditional reserve assets like Treasuries -- demand instead going to gold, commodities, and geopolitical investments. (FT)
11. If less trade is invoiced in U.S. dollars and there is dwindling recycling of dollar surpluses into Treasuries, the "exorbitant privilege" of the dollar as international reserve currency could be under assault. (FT)
