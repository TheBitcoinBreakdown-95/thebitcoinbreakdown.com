## Part 2: Source Inventory

### Core Adversarial Design Principles

1. Bitcoin is money for enemies -- trustless nature and ultra-neutrality means anyone can use it without needing to trust another person. (Author's notes)
2. Bitcoin was designed to work in adversarial environments and be censorship-resistant in the face of state attacks. (Author's notes)
3. Bitcoin is open source -- anyone can recreate it. (Author's notes)
4. Bitcoin runs decentralized, modeled after BitTorrent. (Author's notes)
5. Original use case was facilitating commerce on the dark web; governments could never stop it. (Author's notes)
6. Physical resilience: nodes in Faraday cages, satellites, mesh networks. (Author's notes)
7. Opendimes/USB sticks are more efficient than gold as a value-transfer mechanism. (Author's notes)
8. Hard forks are like a snake shedding its skin -- the network adapts and survives. (Author's notes)
9. Bitcoin is not only secure -- it's the most secure computer network in the history of the world. (Author's notes)

### Bitcoin's Security and Incentive Model

10. Bitcoin's most innovative security aspects aren't purely cryptographic -- they incentivize honest behavior by luring participants into competing for scarce rewards that protect network integrity. (resistance.money)
11. A 51% attack requires Calvin to rebuild the longest chain with more accumulated proof of work -- an enormously expensive task in hardware and energy with no guarantee of success. (resistance.money)
12. Even if a 51% attack succeeds, the cheating itself may undermine the operation's purpose: anyone can see the blockchain, trust collapses, and the value of the spoils sinks below the cost of the attack. (resistance.money)
13. With Bitcoin, cheating doesn't pay. But playing by the rules does -- honest miners with 51% of hashpower make more by behaving honestly. (resistance.money)

### Bitcoin's Undefeated Track Record (Alex Gladstein / Quillette)

14. Bitcoin is undefeated since creation -- price went from $5 to $50,000+; global users eclipsed 100 million. (Gladstein)
15. Tesla, Square, and dozens of companies have added Bitcoin to their corporate treasuries. (Gladstein)
16. Bitcoin has survived both technical (network) attacks and legal (governmental) assaults. (Gladstein)
17. Bitcoin is globally distributed with no central point of failure: no Vitalik Buterin, no foundation, no VC-backing, no pre-mine, no whales able to manipulate the system. (Gladstein)
18. No matter how much Bitcoin you own, you can't change the rules, print more, censor, steal, or prevent others from using the network. (Gladstein)
19. Previous digital currency attempts (e-Gold, Liberty Reserve) were shut down before reaching $10B market cap; Bitcoin now has a $1 trillion+ market cap. (Gladstein)
20. Every day Bitcoin survives, it becomes stronger, and for many attack vectors, the windows are rapidly closing. (Gladstein)
21. Mining, infrastructure, and markets are all independent, in competing jurisdictions, often done by anonymous/pseudonymous actors. (Gladstein)
22. The US Treasury (Mnuchin) attempted a regulatory attack in December 2020 that was defeated by a broad coalition of opposition. (Gladstein)
23. Senator Cynthia Lummis joined the Senate Banking Committee as a Bitcoin supporter, tweeting: "I came for the store of value. I stayed for the censorship-resistance." (Gladstein)

### The 2017 Scaling Wars

24. The New York Agreement (2017) had 83%+ of global mining hashpower, 50+ companies, 20 million wallets -- and was still defeated by grassroots activists. (Gladstein)
25. The lesson: neither miners nor corporations control Bitcoin. Tens of thousands of node operators decide what transactions are valid and what software version is adopted. (Gladstein)
26. Even if a government took control of majority hashrate, it could not change consensus rules, print more Bitcoin, or steal holdings -- only temporarily damage the network. (Gladstein)

### The Cost of a 51% Attack

27. Hardware cost alone for a 51% attack would exceed $5 billion. (Gladstein)
28. Diverting semiconductor fabrication capacity for a 51% attack would put national security at stake during a global chip shortage. (Gladstein)
29. Seizing hundreds of thousands of 5-pound mining machines owned by pseudonymous actors across dozens of jurisdictions would be hugely prohibitive logistically. (Gladstein)

### Technical Attack Vectors and Why They Fail

30. Mining pool transaction censorship: miners make more money from not censoring and can quickly switch to non-censoring pools. (Gladstein)
31. Global internet shutdown: could be disruptive but not fatal to Bitcoin. (Gladstein)
32. Mining hardware backdoors: this actually happened but was not exploited and the threat is fading. (Gladstein)
33. Quantum computers breaking Bitcoin's cryptography: not to be taken seriously according to experts; developers are already working on quantum-resistant algorithms. (Gladstein)
34. Harmful codebase updates: wouldn't stand a chance against hundreds of watchful developers. (Gladstein)

### Legal Attack Vectors

35. Executive Order 6102 (1933): FDR banned gold ownership and forced citizens to hand it in. Bitcoin community prepares for a similar attack via the "Not your keys, not your coins" ethos and annual Proof of Keys day. (Gladstein)
36. A Bitcoin 6102-style seizure would require military home-by-home raids given self-custody via 24 seed words -- constituting mass human rights violations. (Gladstein)
37. Unrealized gains taxes or strict KYC rules face obstacles: First and Fourth Amendment protections, congressional Bitcoin allies, and a large crypto industry lobby. (Gladstein)

### CBDCs Cannot Compete

38. CBDCs like China's DCEP are tied to existing fiat currency and will inevitably fall in relative purchasing power; Bitcoin's purchasing power continues to rise. (Gladstein)
39. No CBDC can offer Bitcoin's level of transactional freedom and privacy from the state. (Gladstein)

### Government Bans Backfire

40. In 2017, China and India restricted citizens' ability to exchange local currency for Bitcoin -- both bans were ineffective and pushed users into peer-to-peer markets. (Gladstein)
41. In Pakistan, Bitcoin is de facto banned but adoption is exploding. (Gladstein)
42. In Nigeria, government threats to freeze bank accounts of Bitcoin buyers drove citizens into harder-to-control peer-to-peer markets. (Gladstein)
43. When governments ban Bitcoin, it ends up accelerating adoption inside their countries. (Gladstein)

### The "Two-Bitcoin" Problem

44. The biggest existing threat: if the top 25 global exchanges ended user withdrawals, it would bifurcate the system into "whitelisted" and "blacklisted" Bitcoin. (Gladstein)
45. Exchanges would lose millions of customers and billions in business; the DeFi ecosystem would collapse; industry would vigorously resist such rules. (Gladstein)

### Bitcoin's Ultimate Defense: Human Nature

46. Governments and elites are self-interested and are more likely to adopt Bitcoin than attack it as it becomes a valuable asset. (Gladstein)
47. Some governments are already mining Bitcoin or encouraging mining -- from Beijing to Kentucky to Siberia to Ukraine. (Gladstein)
48. The genius of Bitcoin is to take advantage of greed and force governments to get involved and help run the system instead of attacking it. (Gladstein)
49. "The only way to kill Bitcoin may be to make it so that people don't need it anymore." (Gladstein)
50. In a world with friendly US regulators, rogue regimes mining Bitcoin, and citizens demanding an inflation-proof asset, the incentive to attack Bitcoin is dwindling. (Gladstein)

### Counterargument Rebuttals (Gladstein Bullet Summary)

51. Price volatility counterargument: Bitcoin's long-term trend has been upward; volatility decreases as the market matures. (Gladstein)
52. Government regulation/ban counterargument: Even in China and India that tried to restrict Bitcoin, usage continued to grow through peer-to-peer markets. (Gladstein)
53. Ethereum flexibility counterargument: Flexibility comes at the cost of decentralization and security; Ethereum's governance can lead to centralization risks as seen in the DAO fork. (Gladstein)
54. Large miners controlling Bitcoin counterargument: Bitcoin's consensus is in the hands of users and node operators, not miners -- proven by the SegWit2X failure. (Gladstein)
55. Quantum computing threat counterargument: Bitcoin's codebase can evolve; quantum-resistant cryptographic algorithms are already being developed. (Gladstein)
56. Two classes of Bitcoin counterargument: Peer-to-peer markets and decentralized exchanges would allow users to bypass whitelisted exchanges, preserving fungibility. (Gladstein)
57. Government collective suppression counterargument: Geopolitical rivalries make this highly unlikely -- nations are more likely to compete to accumulate Bitcoin than destroy it. (Gladstein)

### Game Theory and Decentralization Design

58. Bitcoin needs four things to thrive: miners (security), nodes (decentralization), developers (capability), and buyers (network effect) -- all incentivized by self-interest. (danieleripoll / Twitter thread)
59. Unlike traditional finance, where perverse incentives lead to disproportionate outcomes, Satoshi designed incentives so that pursuing self-interest benefits everyone else on the network. (danieleripoll)
60. Mining decentralization parallels James Madison's Federalist No. 10: the best way to prevent majority rule is geographic dispersal across the world, preventing any single majority from censoring. (Author's notes)
