### Reconstructed Content

The Trezor blog post "Bitcoin and Your Right to Protest" was written in the context of the 2022 Canadian trucker convoy protests. It addresses how Canadian Prime Minister Justin Trudeau invoked emergency powers (the Emergencies Act) to freeze the personal bank accounts of protesters and anyone who donated to them -- without requiring a warrant.

**Key arguments and claims:**

- The overnight seizure of private assets in response to protests is usually attributed to totalitarian regimes, but Canada showed it can happen in any Western democracy.
- If the state becomes corrupt or begins sanctioning peaceful protestors with emergency measures, your money could be seized without a warrant.
- Canadian authorities sanctioned 34 crypto wallets associated with the protests, holding more than $1 million in various cryptocurrencies.
- Some bitcoin donations made to the protests were intercepted because the government targeted the services managing the donations and individuals organizing separate donation drives.
- By March 18, 2022, Canadian police had managed to freeze nearly 6 BTC raised for protesting truckers (out of 21 BTC total donated).
- Bitcoin held in proper self-custody (e.g., a hardware wallet) cannot be seized by the state. Trezor wallets can be used in multisignature setups where several different people each hold a key.
- Bitcoin is a more transparent, predictable way to protect your wealth or raise money for a cause, as long as proper self-custody is maintained.
- The article encourages readers to learn to take custody of their Bitcoin offline and secure their independence against a desperate state.

**Legal aftermath (added context from search):**

- The Federal Court of Canada later ruled that the emergency law giving the government power to stop the flow of funds and crypto to protesting truckers was unreasonable and unconstitutional.

**Key arguments and claims:**

- President Joe Biden issued an executive order directing the federal government to prioritize research into creating a Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) for the United States.
- The executive order stated: "My Administration places the highest urgency on research and development efforts into the potential design and deployment options of a United States CBDC."
- The assessment framework includes examining "possible benefits and risks" alongside "the actions required to launch a United States CBDC if doing so is deemed to be in the national interest."

- A CBDC "could provide households and businesses a convenient, electronic form of central bank money, with the safety and liquidity that would entail; give entrepreneurs a platform on which to create new financial products and services; support faster and cheaper payments (including cross-border payments); and expand consumer access to the financial system."
- The article discusses risks including bank disintermediation (people moving deposits from commercial banks to Fed-held digital dollars) and privacy concerns.
- The Fed paper explicitly noted it would not proceed without support from Congress and the executive branch.

**Note:** The original Wired article likely contained deeper analysis on privacy implications and systemic risk than what the reprints preserved. The core factual content about the EO and Fed paper is captured above.

## Link #6: Ready for the Government to Control How You Spend Your Money? -- American Thinker (Status: PARTIAL)

**URL:** https://www.americanthinker.com/blog/2021/10/ready_for_the_government_to_control_how_you_spend_your_money.html
**Author:** American Thinker staff
**Date:** October 6, 2021
**Scrape method:** WebFetch returned 403. Reconstructed from WebSearch results and related coverage.

### Reconstructed Content

This article was published during the controversy over a provision in Biden's $3.5 trillion spending bill (Build Back Better Act) that would have required financial institutions to report account transactions to the IRS.

**Key arguments and claims:**

- A provision in Biden's spending bill would have required financial institutions to report to the IRS on account transactions (deposits or withdrawals) on accounts totaling more than $600.
- The IRS would be notified every time someone swiped their debit card or wrote a check for more than $600.
- The article characterizes the proposal as "an enormous government data-grab" and "a blatant attempt to control how you spend your money."
- Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen defended the $600 reporting threshold, claiming it was intended to catch tax-dodging billionaires -- a defense critics found absurd given that billionaires do not transact in $600 increments.
- The proposal would vastly expand the IRS's reach, impose huge reporting burdens on banks (driving up costs), and allow the IRS to wander freely through people's bank accounts looking for cash and crimes.
- The proposal was framed as a precursor to broader financial surveillance infrastructure.

**Aftermath (from related coverage):**
- The Biden administration later backed down on the $600 threshold, raising it to $10,000 in annual transactions.
- Income received through a paycheck from which federal taxes are automatically deducted would not be subject to the reporting.
- The episode demonstrated how quickly financial surveillance proposals can emerge and how the infrastructure for monitoring citizen spending is being actively pursued.
- Republican lawmakers introduced the "Prohibiting IRS Financial Surveillance Act" in response.

## Link #7: Crypto Means Absolutely Nothing Without Censorship Resistance -- CoinDesk / George Kaloudis (Status: DONE)

**URL:** https://www.coindesk.com/business/2022/10/23/crypto-means-absolutely-nothing-without-censorship-resistance/
**Author:** George Kaloudis
**Date:** October 23, 2022 (updated June 14, 2024)
**Scrape method:** WebFetch successful.

### Extracted Content

Kaloudis argues that censorship resistance is the foundational value proposition of cryptocurrency, and that removing it undermines the entire purpose of blockchain technology.

**Key arguments and claims:**

**Core Thesis:**
- "Removing censorship resistance defeats the entire purpose and value proposition of cryptocurrency."
- Crypto should enable financial inclusion and access for underserved populations, not replicate existing systems with new technology.

**The FTX/SBF Proposal Problem:**
- Sam Bankman-Fried proposed implementing blocklists and maintaining on-chain OFAC sanctions lists for DeFi.
- While SBF acknowledged that "all of commerce breaks down if you require an allowlist to transact," his proposal to create blocklists was fundamentally contradictory.
- Kaloudis identifies the critical flaw: determining what constitutes "bad" behavior remains subjective and depends on who holds regulatory power. Today's legitimate protest could be tomorrow's sanctioned activity.

**Canadian Trucker Convoy -- Real-World Case Study:**
- The 2022 Canadian trucker protests demonstrated the problem in practice. Though protestors received Bitcoin and Ethereum donations, the transparency of these blockchains made it "practically impossible" to withdraw funds without risking government account freezes.
- This proved that censorship resistance without privacy provides little practical benefit.

**The Privacy Imperative:**
- Censorship resistance alone is insufficient. Bitcoin's public ledger is "quite literally the opposite of anonymous," making transactions traceable.
- A comprehensive solution requires both censorship resistance AND privacy protections.
- Without privacy, transparent blockchains can actually make financial surveillance easier than traditional banking.

**Bennett Tomlin Quote:**
- "If crypto lacks censorship-resistance then it is just gambling and schemes."
- Without censorship resistance and privacy, cryptocurrency offers no meaningful advantage over traditional finance.

- Fiat-backed stablecoin accelerator (for commercial institutions)

- Offers peer-to-peer privacy, scalability, and security.

**Development Pathway (Sandbox to Production):**
1. **Simulation:** Start with an R3-managed sandbox environment
2. **Pilots:** Move to pilots and projects in the central bank's own environment
3. **Production:** Issue, distribute, and exchange a CBDC in production using the "digital mint" solution

**Digital Mint Solution:**
- Enables programming of business rules into the CBDC (programmable money)
- Smart monitoring of money supply
- Corda code available as open source for central banks

**Programmability:**
- CBDCs built on this platform can have business rules programmed directly into the currency itself
- This means the money can be designed to enforce rules about how, when, where, and by whom it can be spent

**Sample Applications:**
- Wholesale CBDC applications (interbank settlement)
- Retail CBDC applications (consumer-facing digital currency)
- Interoperability between networks

**Active Government Projects:**
- **Project Leonidas** (June 2023): Italian Banking Association (ABI) wholesale CBDC trial with the Bank of Italy
- **Project Helvetia Phase III**: SIX and the Swiss National Bank (SNB) piloting wholesale CBDC issuance in Switzerland -- the first orchestration of a real Swiss Franc wCBDC settling digital securities transactions in a live production environment
- **R3 + IDEMIA partnership**: Integrating offline payment capabilities so CBDCs can be used for retail offline transactions

**Significance for Ch 3.3:**
- This is not a theoretical white paper -- it is a production-grade commercial product actively being used by central banks
- The "programmable money" feature is the most concerning element from a freedom perspective: money that enforces rules about how it can be spent
- The sandbox-to-production pipeline shows how CBDCs are being systematically developed and deployed
