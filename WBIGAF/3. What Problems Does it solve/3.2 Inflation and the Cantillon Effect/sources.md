## Link #1 — Wikipedia: Unidade Real de Valor (via WebSearch)

**Source:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unidade_real_de_valor
**Status:** DONE (via WebSearch — Wikipedia blocked WebFetch 403)

The URV (Unidade Real de Valor) was a non-monetary reference currency created in March 1994 as part of the Plano Real in Brazil.

**Key facts:**
- In March 1994, Brazil's inflation rate was nearly 45% per month
- In the first semester of 1994 alone, inflation reached 757%, with a monthly average of 43%
- Inflation exceeded 1,200% per year prior to implementation of the real
- All prices were quoted in two currencies: cruzeiro real and URV
- Payments had to be made exclusively in cruzeiros reais
- Prices quoted in URV did not change over time, while their equivalent in cruzeiros reais increased nominally every day
- The URV worked like a shadow currency with parity to the cruzeiro real constantly adjusted, since it was one-to-one with the dollar
- URV was extinguished July 1, 1994, converted to a new currency (the real) at parity: 1 real = 1 URV = CR$2,750
- Monthly inflation dropped from 48% in June to 7.8% in July to 1.9% in August 1994
- The Plano Real was successful in breaking the hyperinflationary cycle

## Link #8 — EH.net: Economics of the American Revolutionary War

**Source:** https://eh.net/encyclopedia/the-economics-of-the-american-revolutionary-war/
**Status:** DONE (WebFetch)

**Author:** Ben Baack

**Key economic data:**
- Total nominal currency emissions (1775-1779): $241.5 million; total specie value: only $41.02 million
- State currency emissions (1775-1781): approximately $244 million additional
- By year-end 1777, Continental dollar depreciated "to about twenty percent" of original value
- ~90% of Congress's revenue generated from currency emissions
- British executed "a covert program of counterfeiting the Continental dollar" — printed and distributed by British army and Crown loyalists (Newman, 1957)
- Multiple factors accelerated depreciation: rapid money supply expansion, British counterfeiting, market disruption from warfare
- States organized wage and price controls (Rockoff, 1984)
- Congress eventually authorized army to "confiscate whatever it needed"
- "The military effort collapsed before the funding system of Congress" — victory at Yorktown came before financial system completely broke down
- Sugar Act (1764), Stamp Act (1765), Quartering Act (1765), Townshend Acts (1767) — precursors
- Post-war: Constitution granted national government exclusive coining authority — establishing "the institutional foundation for long run growth"
