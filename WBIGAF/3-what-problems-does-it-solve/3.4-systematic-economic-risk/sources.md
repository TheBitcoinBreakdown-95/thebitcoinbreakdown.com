### Core Thesis: Inflation Is Monetary Expansion

Lubka's central argument challenges the narrative that Bitcoin failed as an inflation hedge. He distinguishes between two definitions:

**True inflation (monetary expansion):** "Inflation really is the expansion of the monetary supply," historically defined this way before economists conflated it with consumer price increases.

**CPI (consumer price index):** A separate measure reflecting price increases that may stem from supply shocks rather than monetary expansion.

### Historical Context on the Definition

When the term inflation was first used, it referred to an increase of paper currency against the supply of fixed collateral (gold, silver) that backed the paper notes, meaning that each paper note was worth less in terms of its actual backing. Today, people use inflation to refer to two separate things: "the prices of consumer goods increasing" and "dilution of the money supply."

### Two Scenarios Producing Identical CPI

Lubka uses a thought experiment distinguishing causation:

1. **Supply crisis scenario:** A hypothetical Saudi Arabian oil production halt creates 15% CPI through real scarcity, not money printing
2. **Monetary expansion scenario:** Direct cash transfers ($2,000/month) create identical 15% CPI through demand expansion

His conclusion: "Bitcoin protects investors from the monetary expansion scenario because bitcoin is a monetary good."

### Bitcoin's Actual Performance

During the period of actual monetary expansion (stimulus checks, QE, quantitative easing), Bitcoin performed exceptionally, rising "from 8,000 to 69,000." The subsequent decline occurred during monetary *contraction* — when the Fed ended QE, raised rates, and stock markets fell significantly.

### CPI Can Be Caused by Non-Monetary Factors

Changes in the CPI can be caused by increased money supply, but they are also driven by supply-side changes such as supply chain shocks resulting from the pandemic and more recently the war in Ukraine.

### Stocks as Money Substitutes

Lubka argues equity valuations function similarly to money in modern economies. When the stock market declines substantially, this represents monetary contraction affecting household wealth denominated in dollars, supporting his broader framework about money supply beyond traditional M1/M2 definitions.

### Key Quote

"Bitcoin is the purest inflation hedge because it is just money."

### Bitcoin's Role

You either believe central banks will be forced to continuously expand the money supply or that they won't. If monetary expansion is inevitable, then all Bitcoin needs to do is respond to that monetary expansion better than any other asset — and that is what Bitcoin does better than any other asset.

### The Government's Financial Structure

The U.S. government operates like a business with revenues (primarily taxes) and expenses. About 95% of revenue comes from income, payroll, and excise taxes, with the remaining 5% from estate taxes, customs duties, Federal Reserve earnings, and fees. When spending exceeds revenues, the government operates at a deficit — currently perpetual.

### Defining a Debt Spiral

The mechanism is straightforward: mounting debt requires higher interest payments, which consume an increasing share of tax revenue. When interest expenses exceed available funds after mandatory spending and defense, governments must borrow more, triggering higher rates and deeper deficits. This creates what Lavish calls:

**"More debt -> higher interest rates -> higher deficits -> more debt."**

### America's Key Metrics (2022)

- **Tax revenues:** $4.8 trillion
- **Mandatory expenses:** $3.7 trillion (entitlements)
- **Defense spending:** $800 billion
- **Interest expense:** $400 billion annually
- **Interest coverage ratio:** 0.75x (revenues minus entitlements and defense divided by interest)

### The Alarming Conclusion

"Entitlement spending + interest on debt > revenues."

At 3.2% debt replacement rates, annual interest costs could reach $1 trillion, pushing the coverage ratio to 0.3x — comparable to bankrupt corporations.

### The Fed's Dilemma

Central banks face an impossible choice: lower rates (triggering inflation) or accept higher borrowing costs. Either path perpetuates the cycle.

### The Spiral Mechanism in Detail

When you max out credit and are still not meeting obligations, when you go out to take out another credit card, the interest rate is even higher. The interest rates go up on credit card borrowing while monthly earnings are not keeping up. Higher interest rates on US Treasuries being auctioned cause the Interest On Debt number to rise, widening the deficit gap even more — the dreaded Debt Spiral.

The situation is that interest payments are going up and tax revenues are going down — a spiral. Because the US Government spends far more than it makes, it has to keep borrowing, and with interest rates rising, the spending deficit has only widened with higher interest expense.

### Context Update (2024)

Lavish noted in September 2024: "For those wondering what a Debt Spiral is, I wrote all about it two years ago, when US Debt was at $30.7 trillion. We are now at $35.4 trillion, and growing...."

### Recommended Protection

Lavish advises allocating portfolio portions to hard assets — gold, silver, and Bitcoin — as hedges against eventual fiat currency depreciation or collapse.

### Structural Inflationary Forces: The Four Rs

The combination of "reshoring," "restocking," "rearming" and "rebuilding" will create a new structurally inflationary force that is immune to the business cycle, as the outlays are strategically necessary to support national security imperatives.

National leaders now know that critical commodity inputs and finished goods supply chains dominated by hostile or politically antagonistic nations can and will be weaponized. Western multinationals that have only known the deflationary cost-savings from globalization will confront a great reversion of reshoring, which will be strongly encouraged, if not required, by overt sovereign industrial policies.

### Risk to the Current Geopolitical Order

These meso-scale forces will interact constructively with micro-scale variables of UST market weakness, commodity-finance fragility, and associated macroeconomic fragility to place the current geopolitical order at high risk of fracture.

### Power and Money

Money is an expression of power, and the power to decide what is money — and who can or cannot use it — is a unique and consequential power. Given the current geopolitical crisis, the imminent contest for global power may unfold as the deciding factor shaping the evolution of the global system and its associated monetary regime.

### The Value of Domestic Reserves

The value of domestic reserves (of hard resources, not mere foreign exchange), of domestic production capacity and of reliable and "friendly" supply chains now have geopolitical premiums.

### De-dollarization and Reserve Diversification

Exporting nations (e.g., OPEC+) with large existing UST and eurodollar foreign exchange holdings may decide to diversify on the margin away from those reserves to recycle excess surpluses into a different basket of non-G7 inside money and outside money (e.g., gold, commodity stockpiles, and eventually Bitcoin).

### Bitcoin's Role in the U.S.

In the United States, the rise of Bitcoin companies (especially miners) as economically and soon politically powerful entities will likely mitigate any extreme federal government response, and will result in pro-Bitcoin coalitions at the local and state level.

### Bitcoin as a New Reserve Asset

Bitcoin is emerging as a viable alternative to fiat currencies and gold as a global reserve asset. It is a unique form of money: a digital commodity with global fungibility, limited counterparty risk, and large liquidity.

### What Is a CDS?

A CDS (Credit Default Swap) functions as an insurance contract between two parties. One party underwrites protection against a borrower's default, while another purchases this coverage. If the borrower defaults, the CDS issuer compensates the buyer.

The CDS market operates transparently, with prices reflecting investor expectations about default probabilities. As default risk increases, insurance costs rise accordingly.

### Historical Precedent: The Big Short

The article references the film *The Big Short*, noting how "a group of hedge fund managers who saw the housing crash coming used Credit Default Swaps (CDS) to make a fortune."

### Greg Foss's Core Thesis

Greg Foss, a 35-year veteran of the credit markets and an accomplished risk analyst with some of Canada's most prominent financial institutions, has said:

**"Bitcoin is the best asymmetric trade I have ever seen."**

Foss thinks Bitcoin should be considered default insurance on the entire global fiat currency system — like a CDS on the US dollar, Canadian dollar, British pound, euro, yen, yuan, and all the rest of the government currencies.

### The Valuation Model

If Bitcoin is like a CDS on the entire fiat currency system, then we can use the data in the CDS market to create a fair price valuation for Bitcoin. According to the model presented by Giambruno, the estimated cost to insure all US federal government obligations against default is about $2.98 trillion, which means if Bitcoin is like a CDS on the global fiat currency system, the fair value of all outstanding Bitcoin should be at least $2.98 trillion or $154,390 per BTC at the then-current supply.

### Lebanon Case Study

The article uses Lebanon as a case study, showing how someone with $100,000 USD equivalent in lira savings could have converted $50,000 into Bitcoin at $8,333 per BTC, acquiring about 6 Bitcoins that could be moved anywhere without depending on banks or governments.

### Forward Application

The CDS market will again telegraph critical information for shrewd investors to exploit during the anticipated coming financial crisis. Bitcoin functions as the ultimate CDS — one that doesn't require a counterparty and can't be frozen or seized.

### Core Argument

In the 1970s, Paul Volcker controlled inflation by raising interest rates to very high levels (the Fed funds rate peaked at 19.1% in June 1981). However, the current situation is fundamentally different because of total debt levels, debt as a percentage of GDP, and the cost of servicing that debt.

### The Volcker Shock — What Happened Then

- Fed funds rate rose from an average of 11.2% in 1979 to a peak of 20% in June 1981
- Inflation rates dropped from a peak of ~14.8% in March 1980 to ~3.8% by 1983
- The cost: a sharp recession with unemployment reaching nearly 11%
- Triggered Latin American debt crises as governments defaulted on USD-denominated debts

### Why It Can't Work Now — The Math

**Debt-to-GDP comparison:**
- Volcker era (1980): Debt-to-GDP ratio was approximately 26-35%
- Today: Debt-to-GDP ratio is ~125-128% — more than three and a half times higher

**Interest expense sensitivity:**
- If the interest rate rises by 100 basis points, the average annual interest cost would increase by ~$187 billion
- During the 1980s and 1990s, interest payments as a share of the national debt averaged 2.7% of GDP

**Luke Gromen's assessment:**
"This is more about math than Fed Chair resolution: with federal debt-to-GDP at 125% and US tax receipts set to fall considerably, this is a matter of the US government's solvency — not the sitting Fed Chair."

### The Fed Is Trapped

The conclusion: the Federal Reserve cannot simply replicate Volcker's approach today. The dramatically different economic landscape — particularly regarding debt levels and debt servicing costs relative to GDP — would make aggressive rate hikes far more damaging to the federal budget and overall economic stability than they were in the early 1980s. The Fed's only realistic option is to eventually pivot back to accommodation and accept higher structural inflation.

### The Four Types of Money

According to economic literature, there are four distinct types of money:

1. **Commodity money:** Derives value from intrinsic worth — precious metals like gold and silver. Has maintained value across civilizations.
2. **Fiat money:** Government-issued currency with no commodity backing. Value by decree. Today's dominant system.
3. **Fiduciary money:** Face value exceeds material worth, but maintains partial backing through institutional promises (e.g., early banknotes).
4. **Commercial bank money:** Created through fractional reserve banking — institutions create new money by issuing loans against deposits.

### Historical Pattern of Fiat Failure

All fiat currencies go to zero in purchasing power over time. Historical examples include:
- Roman denarius
- German Reichsmark (Weimar Republic)
- Hungarian pengo (mid-1940s)
- Chilean peso (1970s-1980s)
- Zimbabwean dollar
- Venezuelan bolivar
- Belarusian ruble (early 1990s, late 2000s)

### What Happens When Fiat Fails

When fiat currency collapses, economies naturally gravitate toward more stable forms of money:
- **Foreign currency** (usually USD in developing countries — but what if USD itself weakens?)
- **Commodities** (gold, silver, oil)
- **Cryptocurrency** (Bitcoin as "digital gold")

People will search for alternative money if too much money is printed. In cases of political turmoil or economic mismanagement, public trust in the monetary system can erode, leading to a loss of value for the currency.

### Bitcoin as the Post-Fiat Alternative

Unlike fiat money, Bitcoin has a fixed supply — only 21 million coins will ever exist. Bitcoin's fixed supply and decentralized nature offer a consistent frame of reference for everyone to see the devaluation of fiat currencies in real-time. Similar to commodity money, Bitcoin has a deflationary and fixed supply — called "digital gold" because of its designed scarcity.

The rising adoption of Bitcoin signals a collective move towards a money that stands firm in the face of inflationary pressures and is free of manipulation.
