## The Oldest Trick in Civilization

Take Rome. The Roman denarius started out as a serious coin... over 90% pure silver in the first century AD.[2] The kind of money you could trust. The kind of money that held its value because it *was* the value.

Then the emperors got creative.

Nero kicked it off in 64 CE. After the Great Fire of Rome, he needed to rebuild the city and fund his lavish Golden House. So he shaved the silver content from 3.9 grams to 3.4.[3] Not enough for anyone to notice right away. Just enough to squeeze out a few more coins from the same pile of silver.

Sound familiar?

Septimius Severus made it an art form. Around 200 CE he slashed the silver content to 40-50%, using the "savings" to triple his soldiers' pay.[4] Buying loyalty with debased currency. Exactly like modern governments buying votes with stimulus checks.

And the pattern kept going. By 300 AD, the denarius had plummeted to 5% silver. By the end... 2%. Essentially a copper coin with a silver wash.[5]

"The percentage of silver in the common denarius pretty accurately mirrors the state of the Roman economy... and the stability of Rome overall."[6]

Three hundred years. That's how long it took Rome to inflate itself into collapse. The only thing that changes across civilizations is the speed. Rome took 300 years. Weimar took 3.[7]

## Meet Richard Cantillon

His name was Richard Cantillon. Irish-French economist, born sometime in the 1680s, died under suspicious circumstances in 1734. He wrote one book... *Essai sur la Nature du Commerce en Général*... and it was published posthumously in 1755. William Stanley Jevons would later call it "the cradle of political economy," the first complete treatise on economics.[9]

Cantillon figured out something that should have changed everything.

When new money enters an economy, it doesn't reach everyone at the same time. The first receivers... the banks, the financiers, the politically connected... they get to spend it at the old prices. Before the market has adjusted. Before the prices rise. And by the time that new money trickles down to ordinary people, to the workers, to the savers, to the ones who actually produce things, prices have already gone up.[10]

The first receivers benefit. The last receivers pay.

That's it. That's the Cantillon Effect. And here's the kicker... Cantillon didn't just theorize about it. He *lived* it. He made an enormous fortune by understanding the inflationary dynamics of John Law's Mississippi Company scheme in the 1710s and 1720s, positioning himself to profit while his debtors were destroyed.[11]

The people he ruined came after him with lawsuits, criminal charges, and murder plots until the day he died.[12]

The true trickle down economics is the Cantillon Effect.[13] And it's been operating for 300 years. The man who named it lived and died by it. Nothing has changed.

## The Rigged Ruler

Now here's where it gets really infuriating.

The government looked at this disaster... and decided to change the way it measures inflation.

CPI. The Consumer Price Index. It's a government basket of goods and services that's supposed to track how much more expensive life is getting.[48] And it's extremely misleading. But it's not technically wrong. Because the government keeps changing what "wrong" means.

In 1996, the Boskin Commission concluded that CPI was *overstating* inflation by about 1.1 percentage points per year.[49] Overstating. The government's own commission looked at the data and said, you know what, things aren't actually getting as expensive as we thought. So they recommended three major changes.

First: substitution bias. When steak gets too expensive, the Bureau of Labor Statistics now assumes you buy hamburger instead.[50] And records it as unchanged cost of living. They literally redefined inflation to measure the cost of *surviving*, not the cost of maintaining your standard of living.

Second: hedonic quality adjustments. Your computer costs the same as last year, but it has a faster processor. The BLS records that as a price *decrease*.[51] Applied to electronics, vehicles, appliances... always pulling measured inflation downward.

Third: in 1999, the BLS changed the entire CPI formula from a Laspeyres index to geometric means, building the assumption of consumer substitution directly into the math.[52]

Under pre-1980 methodology, the kind John Williams at ShadowStats still calculates, inflation during 2020-2022 was closer to 15%. Not the reported 7-9%.[53]

And here's why the government did it. Every percentage point of "reduced bias" saves the government billions in Social Security obligations.[54] Billions. Lower measured inflation means lower cost-of-living adjustments. Lower COLA means smaller checks. Smaller checks mean smaller deficits. On paper.

The ruler changed. Not the reality.[55]

If you measure inflation the way the government measured it in 1980, the dollar has lost purchasing power roughly twice as fast as the official narrative suggests. You didn't get 3% inflation. You got 7.5%.[56] You just didn't know because they changed the thermometer.
