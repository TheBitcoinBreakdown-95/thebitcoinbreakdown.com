## Part 2: Source Inventory

### Core Game Theory Architecture

1. Bitcoin is a series of game-theoretic feedback loops that embed it further and further into society. (Author's notes)
2. Bitcoin was designed with human nature in mind -- to hijack our greed and increase its price forever. (Author's notes)
3. Increases in price secure the network even more -- it will slowly absorb all of the wealth in the world. (Author's notes)
4. Unlike normal assets where rising prices incentivize more production and push prices back down, Bitcoin's supply schedule is fixed -- when price goes up, miners try to mine more but can't make more coins, so the price stays up. (Author's notes)
5. The mining process confirms transactions and secures the network -- a positive feedback loop that makes the protocol more secure and valuable, raises the price, and the process repeats. (Author's notes)
6. "I came for the 'getting rich' but I stayed for the 'global monetary disruption.'" (Author's notes)
7. Bitcoin uses financial incentives to gain notice and adoption even without a founder or marketing team. (Author's notes)

### Bitcoin's Four Survival Needs (danieleripoll / Twitter thread)

8. Bitcoin needs four things to stay alive and thrive: miners (security/immutability), nodes (decentralization), developers (capabilities), and buyers (network effect). (danieleripoll)
9. "As brilliantly as Satoshi designed the protocol itself, he also demonstrated next level genius by engineering the incentives to make sure the network would thrive all on its own." (danieleripoll)

### Miner Incentives

10. Miners must invest their own money to build energy infrastructure and equipment to turn energy into hashes -- the more hashes, the more BTC earned from block rewards and fees. (danieleripoll)
11. The block reward was a brilliant way to incentivize investment in the early days before Bitcoin had massive network effect -- in expectation of an outsized return. (danieleripoll)
12. Despite price being more than 60% off all-time high and fees not covering miner costs, the network hashrate exploded from 160 EH to 400 EH at the time of writing -- indicating a global hash war to acquire the last 2 million coins. (danieleripoll)
13. As miners pursue selfish interests, everyone else benefits from additional network security. (danieleripoll)

### Node Incentives and Decentralization

14. For the first time in history, we have a form of decentralized open-source money truly beyond the reach of governments and central banks. (danieleripoll)
15. Running a node gives people a say in protocol development, empowers them as guardians of the protocol rules, and enhances transaction verification and privacy. (danieleripoll)
16. The more people who run nodes, the more decentralized Bitcoin becomes -- and the more impervious it is to attack or co-option. (danieleripoll)

### Developer Incentives and Layer 2

17. By focusing on security and decentralization on layer 1, the core protocol remains simple and robust -- scaling must happen on higher layers. (danieleripoll)
18. Restricting block size gives builders confidence that Bitcoin will imbue the same key store-of-value properties in 20 years that it has today. (danieleripoll)
19. Bitcoin's layer 1 constraints represent opportunities for entrepreneurs to add value on higher layers without sacrificing security or decentralization. (danieleripoll)
20. Lightning Network solves what Bitcoin cannot solve on the base layer (scalability); Fedimint adds capabilities that Lightning cannot easily solve (simplified non-custodial experience). (danieleripoll)
21. All these capabilities come to Bitcoin without costing Bitcoin any money -- no foundation or active founder required. (danieleripoll)

### Buyer Network Effects

22. Bitcoin solves a real-world problem for nearly everyone on earth -- plebs, elites, institutions, and nation states all have an incentive to increase their purchasing power. (danieleripoll)
23. Bitcoin has proven itself to be the fastest horse in the race against inflation -- the more it performs, the more FOMO drives adoption, propelling price further and strengthening network effect. (danieleripoll)
24. Higher price gives miners growing incentive to invest more in securing the network, gives more people incentive to run a node, and gives more companies incentive to build products on top of Bitcoin. (danieleripoll)

### Supply Dynamics and Price Mechanics

32. The market cap is BS and has nothing to do with the demand or size of the network -- the price is determined by the marginal seller, impacted by supply and demand. (Author's notes)
33. Demand held equal, supply will continue to decline, signaling improvement in price -- supply hard-coded via difficulty adjustment and halving events. (Author's notes)
34. Liquid supply is impacted by those willing to sell -- if Bitcoin is held as a store of value and more is taken off exchanges never to be sold, the supply crunch will be more intense than the predictable stock-to-flow. (Author's notes)
35. Bitcoin is unique in that supply is already extremely limited and there is a culture/religion around never selling. (Author's notes)
36. Supply is impacted by coins that are burned, coins staked into other crypto markets, and coins locked into smart contracts and lending as collateral. (Author's notes)
37. The faster the price appreciation, the less supply will be available since gains are accelerating and short-term investors have greater opportunity cost to sell. (Author's notes)
38. Traditional investment vehicles like ETFs correlated 1:1 with Bitcoin mean more Bitcoin must be locked into funds, making it inaccessible to exchanges. (Author's notes)
39. International adoption as a store of value and inflation hedge represents hundreds of millions of prime adopters whose local currencies and investment options are far inferior to Bitcoin. (Author's notes)
40. "I think all price prediction models are conservative and this thing is gonna pop the fuck off." (Author's notes)

### Nash Equilibrium and Schelling Point

41. "Bitcoin's terminal money supply growth (inflation) rate of absolute zero is the ultimate monetary Schelling point -- a game-theoretic focal point that people tend to choose in an adversarial game." (Breedlove / Medium)
42. Nash equilibrium is a concept where the optimal outcome of a game is one where no player has an incentive to deviate from their chosen strategy after considering an opponent's choice. (Investopedia)
43. Money converges into one -- in game theory that's called Nash Equilibrium. It's when the most people are benefited. (Author's notes)

### Hodler Network Effects

44. The more hodlers, the more rich they all get -- the more powerful they get, the more people with a vested interest in keeping the system alive. Security increases not just in the miner sphere but also in the social and regulatory sphere as people get richer. (Author's notes)
45. Lindy effect/time: the longer Bitcoin survives, the more likely it is to not go away. Market cap/liquidity increase means more high-net-worth individuals adopt (since less volatile and less risky), increasing legitimacy, liquidity, and performance, which brings in more money. (Author's notes)

### Deflation Psychology

46. The deflation feedback loop causes major psychological FOMO -- every single bitcoiner goes through the same thought: I joined too late, and I wish I had bought more. (Author's notes)

### Mining Decentralization as Governance

47. There is a game theory of mining decentralization -- similar to James Madison's writings in Federalist No. 10 on majority faction. Geographic dispersal of mining across the world prevents any single majority from censoring or disrupting Bitcoin's ability to function. (Author's notes)
