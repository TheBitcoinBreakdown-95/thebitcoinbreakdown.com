## L1: Orthography & Visual Style

**Bitcoin/bitcoin distinction:** The author generally follows the convention of uppercase "Bitcoin" for the network/protocol and lowercase "bitcoin" for the currency unit, though application is inconsistent. In *Absolute Scarcity*: "Bitcoin has value because it is absolutely scarce" (network) vs "there is a cap of 21000000 bitcoins" (units). However, the rule blurs frequently — "bitcoin is the hardest money" (should arguably be uppercase since referring to the system). This inconsistency itself is a voice marker: the author cares about the convention but applies it loosely, erring toward lowercase in casual passages.

**Emphasis via bold and italic:** Bold is used sparingly but deliberately for single-word or phrase emphasis, not for headers within prose. In *Intro*: "it's a **really** tough subject to explain." Italics are used more broadly, often for entire phrases that carry tonal weight. In *The Trust Machine*: "*The entire point of bitcoin* is to remove the distribution, production, and control over money from humanity." In *Absolute Scarcity*: "*We're talking* pristine international collateral!"

**ALL CAPS:** Used occasionally for emotional emphasis, never for headers. In *Designed Money*: "EVERY TIME ITS TRIED" and "AND YET" and "BILLIONS" and "MAYBE." In *Bitcoin Will Save the World*: no ALL CAPS — the emphasis there comes from sentence structure instead. ALL CAPS is reserved for moments of exasperation or incredulity.

**Headings style:** Major section headings tend to be bold and sometimes phrased as questions ("But but but, there are thousands of other coins?!" in *Absolute Scarcity*) or declarative statements ("Bitcoin is Physics in Cyberspace" in *The Trust Machine*). The author also uses headings as rhetorical pivot points, not just organizational markers.

**Spelling/regional:** American English throughout. Minor typos and casual errors are present and not corrected, suggesting the writing is closer to speech than edited prose: "its" instead of "it's" in *Intro* ("If I'm good at one thing its organizing knowledge"), "loose" instead of "lose" in *Absolute Scarcity* ("doesn't loose it's value"), "Your" instead of "You're" in *Intro* ("You're journey begins"). These are consistent patterns — the author writes fast and doesn't heavily copyedit.

**Formatting patterns:** Heavy use of bullet lists for stacked claims, especially in *Bitcoin Will Save the World* and *Designed Money*. Lists are used when the author wants to rapid-fire multiple points. Images break up long stretches of text at regular intervals (roughly every 3-5 paragraphs).

## L2: Punctuation & Micro-Prosody

**Commas:** The author underuses commas relative to formal English. Run-on sentences are common and feel deliberate — they create breathless momentum. From *Absolute Scarcity*: "This fixed supply is the reason bitcoin is the perfect store of value, and is the best savings technology ever invented." The comma before "and" varies — sometimes present, sometimes absent. Oxford comma is inconsistently applied.

**Em dashes and hyphens:** Em dashes are almost entirely absent. The author uses hyphens and occasional dashes but does not deploy em dashes as a stylistic punctuation device. This is notable — many opinion writers lean heavily on em dashes. Instead, this author prefers to start a new sentence or use a comma.

**Ellipses:** Used as trailing-off markers, informal and conversational. In *Intro*: "I started off like everyone else, thinking it would either make me rich, or that it was cute nerd money that would just fade away someday..." and "You're journey begins...with a simple question.." (note the inconsistent dot count — two dots instead of three). Ellipses signal the author thinking aloud or creating suspense.

**Exclamation marks:** Used with restraint but present for genuine enthusiasm or mockery. In *Designed Money*: "Anyone can literally just make up their own money!" and "trust me it'll be really valuable someday!!!!" (four exclamation marks for sarcastic mockery). In *Absolute Scarcity*: "But but but, there are thousands of other coins?!" — the combo of ?! signals feigned indignation. Exclamation marks are tied to emotional peaks, not habitual.

**Question marks:** Questions are a major rhetorical device, but they tend to be genuine-seeming ("How can I trust that when I send you a bitcoin, you won't cheat me and just spend it twice?" in *The Trust Machine*) or mock-naive ("Why does it have any value at all?" in *Designed Money*). The author doesn't stack rapid-fire questions — they pose one and then answer it.

**Semicolons:** Almost completely absent across all five posts. This is a strong marker — the author writes in a way that avoids subordinate-clause complexity. The rhythm is short declarative sentences and comma splices, not semicolon-joined clauses.

**Colons:** Used before lists or elaborations, but informally. In *Absolute Scarcity*: "Bitcoin has value because it is absolutely scarce - there is a cap of 21000000 bitcoins" — note the hyphen where a colon might go. The author prefers hyphens and dashes over colons for inline explanations.

**Periods as rhythm:** The author frequently uses very short sentences as standalone paragraph-ending punches. "This is bitcoin's unique value proposition." "This is the trust machine." "Absolute scarcity." These are full-stop drops used for emphasis.

## L4: Syntax & Sentence Geometry

**Sentence length distribution:** Highly variable, ranging from 2-word fragments ("Absolute scarcity.") to 60+ word run-ons. The author favors long explanatory sentences broken by very short declarative punches. From *The Trust Machine*: "Bitcoin is the money and the network. It's both the matter and the gravity." — short, parallel, punchy. Then immediately followed by a longer elaboration.

**Fragments for emphasis:** Frequently used. "But for good reason." (*The Trust Machine*). "Not so with bitcoin." (*Absolute Scarcity*). "Absolute scarcity." (*Absolute Scarcity*). "The separation of money and state." (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*). "The perfect money." (*Designed Money*). Fragments appear at rhetorical peaks — they are the mic-drop moments.

**Sentence-initial words:** The author starts sentences with conjunctions constantly:
- "And" — "And after 15 years..." (*Absolute Scarcity*), "And the corruption that comes with it." (*The Trust Machine*)
- "But" — "But this is why this 'magic beans' argument confused me" (*Designed Money*), "But because the only thing you can do with bitcoin is trade it" (*Designed Money*)
- "So" — "So for those who can't see the utility right away" (*Designed Money*)
- "Or" — "Or at least those late to the bitcoin party." (*Designed Money*)

Starting with "And" or "But" is one of the most frequent syntactic patterns — it creates a conversational, building rhythm.

**Active voice dominance:** The author overwhelmingly uses active voice. Passive constructions are rare and typically appear in technical descriptions ("Transactions are recorded on an immutable ledger" in *The Trust Machine*). The default mode is subject-verb-object: "Bitcoin removes humans from the control over money." "Satoshi designed bitcoin to be decentralized."

**"It's" constructions:** The author frequently starts or pivots sentences with "It's" — "It's a defensive technology," "It's money that can't be debased," "It's a protest," "It's a science fiction nightmare." This is a signature construction — the pronoun "it" referring to bitcoin, followed by a bold characterization.

**Parallel lists within sentences:** The author chains descriptors with commas: "trustless, and absolutely scarce" / "decentralized, trustless, and absolutely scarce" / "censorship resistant, permissionless, and available to all of humanity." These triple-beat lists are a rhythmic signature.

**Second person direct address:** The author speaks directly to "you" frequently: "You should not be taking things at face value" (*Intro*), "I bet you didn't know this" (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*), "I want you to know" (*Intro*). This is intimate and instructor-like.

**First person confessional:** The author regularly inserts personal history and emotional stakes: "Ever since I was 13 year's old" (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*), "It took me a hundred hours to understand it" (*Intro*), "This was actually my personal lightbulb moment" (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*), "my anarchist roommate at summer camp" (*Designed Money*). These personal asides are irregular but powerful — they humanize the author.

## L5: Cadence & Paragraph Choreography

**One-sentence paragraphs:** Extremely common and a defining rhythm feature. Examples:
- "This is bitcoin's unique value proposition." (*Absolute Scarcity*)
- "That's where Bitcoin comes in." (*The Trust Machine*)
- "This is the trust machine." (*The Trust Machine*)
- "I'm passionate about bitcoin because it's literally our only hope." (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*)
- "That power should never be trusted with the human race." (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*)
- "The answer however, might make you go mad." (*Designed Money*)

These one-liners serve as punctuation between longer explanatory passages. They are the author's primary tool for emphasis — a thesis statement dropped alone on a line.

**Paragraph length pattern:** The dominant rhythm is: 1-3 sentences of context/explanation -> one-sentence paragraph drop -> 2-4 sentences of elaboration -> another one-liner. This creates a heartbeat-like rhythm of build-punch-build-punch.

**Long-then-short for impact:** This is the author's signature move. A dense analytical paragraph about monetary theory will be followed immediately by something like "Not so with bitcoin." or "Bitcoin has no competition." The long paragraph provides the intellectual setup; the short follow-up delivers the emotional payload.

**White space:** The author uses white space generously. Paragraphs rarely exceed 4-5 sentences. Dense walls of text are broken by images, quotes, or one-liners. The visual impression is airy and readable, not academic.

**Build-up to thesis statements:** The author often builds through 3-5 paragraphs of evidence/argument before landing on a bold declarative: "Because of bitcoin's terminal supply and halving events, bitcoin is the hardest money that has ever existed." (*Absolute Scarcity*). The payoff sentence is always shorter and simpler than what precedes it.

**Section endings:** Posts tend to end with forward-looking statements or challenges, not summaries. *Intro* ends with "What is bitcoin?" — a question. *Absolute Scarcity* ends with a link to the next post. *The Trust Machine* ends with "THAT is monetary revolution." *Bitcoin Will Save the World* ends with a massive list of technologies. This author does not wrap things up neatly — the ending propels you forward.

## L6: Discourse Strategy & Rhetorical Moves

**Opening strategies:** Three distinct patterns:
1. **Quote or maxim opener:** *Absolute Scarcity* opens with an italicized aphorism ("Money doesn't grow on trees, because if it did we would call it leaves."). *The Trust Machine* opens with a Satoshi quote.
2. **Direct command/challenge:** *Intro* opens with imperative sentences: "Forget everything you know about bitcoin. Forget everything you know about blockchain, and crypto."
3. **Devil's advocate / steelman the opposition:** *Designed Money* opens by voicing the skeptic's position: "All bitcoin is, is fake money. Why does it have any value at all?" The author inhabits the critic's voice before dismantling it.

**Steelmanning the counterargument:** This is a major and distinctive rhetorical move. The author doesn't strawman objections — he voices them in their strongest form, often with humor. "But but but, there are thousands of other coins?!" (*Absolute Scarcity*). The entire opening section of *Designed Money* (roughly 300 words) is written FROM the skeptic's perspective before the pivot: "This is what everyone thinks the first time they learn about bitcoin."

**The pivot/reveal:** After building context or steelmanning objections, the author executes a sharp pivot, typically a one-sentence paragraph:
- "That's where Bitcoin comes in." (*The Trust Machine*)
- "Bitcoin is Designed Money." (*Designed Money*)
- "I bet no one has ever told you this, but this is what bitcoin is really all about." (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*)

**Analogy as explanation:** The author's primary explanatory device is analogy, not technical definition. Rather than explain the blockchain technically, he calls it "The great excel spreadsheet in the sky" and then upgrades the metaphor to "a golden tablet in the clouds" (*The Trust Machine*). Rather than explain scarcity abstractly, he says "the Mona Lisa, or a plot of land in Manhattan, or Wu-Tang Clan's *Once Upon a Time in Shaolin* broken up into a couple quadrillion little pieces" (*Absolute Scarcity*). Analogies come in threes or escalating sequences.

**Quote integration:** External quotes are used frequently and serve as authority-lending devices. Satoshi Nakamoto is quoted most often, followed by financial figures (Paul Tudor Jones, Stanley Druckenmiller) and Bitcoin thinkers (Andreas Antonopoulos, Robert Breedlove, GiGi, Lyn Alden). Quotes are introduced with casual phrasing: "In the wise words of Satoshi:" (*The Trust Machine*). They are used to validate a point already made, never as the primary argument.

**Pronoun shifts (he/she for Satoshi):** In *The Trust Machine*, the author alternates between "he" and "she" when referring to Satoshi: "he had to invent digital scarcity. He had to put physics into the internet. She had to recreate the law of conservation of energy." This deliberate alternation signals awareness of Satoshi's unknown identity and is a distinctive voice choice.

**Closing strategies:**
- Forward link to next post (*Intro*, *Absolute Scarcity*, *Bitcoin Will Save the World*)
- Grand declarative statement: "THAT is monetary revolution" (*The Trust Machine*)
- Lists of implications or projects (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*)
- The author does NOT summarize or recapitulate. Endings are propulsive, not reflective.

**Personal narrative as hook:** The author regularly inserts autobiographical moments to ground abstract arguments: hearing about Bitcoin through the Silk Road, the anarchist roommate at summer camp, being obsessed with the federal debt since age 13, the "lightbulb moment." These are deployed at moments when the argument might feel too abstract — they pull the reader back to a human scale.

**The "you don't realize" move:** A recurring rhetorical gesture is telling the reader they don't know something important. "I bet you didn't know this" (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*). "There's a lot more to bitcoin then you might realize." (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*). "The sectors that bitcoin aims to disrupt are MUCH larger than you could possibly know" (*Absolute Scarcity*). This creates a sense of hidden knowledge being unveiled.

## L7: Epistemic Posture & Thinking Style

**Confidence level: Very high, but earned through personal journey.** The author makes sweeping, bold claims: "bitcoin is the hardest money that has ever existed," "the most important project in the world," "literally our only hope." These are not hedged. However, the confidence is tempered by the author's repeated admission of his own past skepticism: "I too was skeptical about bitcoin for many years" (*Intro*), "I assumed it was a failed experiment" (*Designed Money*). The message is: I was where you are, I did the work, and now I'm certain.

**Hedging language: Rare.** Modal verbs like "might" and "could" appear almost exclusively in technical or market-prediction contexts: "the rate at which the price increase could be exponential" (*Absolute Scarcity*). The author does not hedge on bitcoin's importance or fundamental properties. The absence of "I think" before major claims is notable — the author states, he does not speculate.

**Reader relationship: Teacher-evangelist, not academic-peer.** The author positions himself as someone slightly ahead on the same journey: "My job is to make your fall down the rabbit hole as easy as possible" (*Intro*). He does not claim academic or institutional authority. He claims experiential authority — hundreds of hours of research. The tone is that of an older sibling who found something incredible and is urgently trying to explain it.

**Skepticism as a value:** Despite the high confidence, the author repeatedly promotes skepticism as a virtue: "don't trust, verify" (cited in *Intro*, *The Trust Machine*, and *Designed Money*), "Do not trust what you read without rationalizing it for yourself" (*Intro*), "I also understand that it's vital you don't blindly trust me" (*Designed Money*). This creates an interesting tension: the author is deeply convinced but insists the reader should be skeptical. The resolution is that the author believes the evidence speaks for itself — skepticism will lead you to the same conclusion.

**Emotional honesty:** The author names his emotions directly: "I'm passionate about bitcoin" (*Intro*), "I'm passionate about bitcoin because it's literally our only hope" (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*), "This was actually my personal lightbulb moment" (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*). There's no emotional distance or cool detachment. The author cares and shows it.

**Binary framing:** The author frequently presents choices as stark binary outcomes: "Either bitcoin succeeds or the human race irrevocably falls into a technocratic dystopia" (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*). "Bitcoin has no competition" (*Absolute Scarcity*). "Once you understand that bitcoin has no competition, its unique value proposition of absolute scarcity will cut through the noise" (*Absolute Scarcity*). This is not qualified — it is presented as the logical conclusion of the evidence.

**Relationship to existing knowledge:** The author positions Bitcoin knowledge as hidden or suppressed, not as commonly known: "I bet no one has ever told you this" (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*), "no one seems to understand or give a shit" (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*). There is a strong "red pill" undercurrent — the author sees himself as helping people see what they're blind to, especially in the West.

**Authority citations:** The author leans on a specific canon: Satoshi Nakamoto, Austrian economics (Ludwig von Mises), Bitcoin thought leaders (Andreas Antonopoulos, Robert Breedlove, GiGi, Lyn Alden), and institutional validation (Fidelity, BlackRock). He does not cite mainstream economists or conventional academic sources. The knowledge base is self-taught and community-sourced.

## Signature Sentences

These sentences are the most characteristic of this author's voice — they capture the tone, rhythm, structure, and worldview in concentrated form:

1. "Forget everything you know about bitcoin. Forget everything you know about blockchain, and crypto." (*Intro*) — Imperative, repetitive, dramatic opening.

2. "It's the same reason air is free and a Denny's Grand Slam isn't." (*Absolute Scarcity*) — Highbrow concept grounded with a lowbrow reference.

3. "You can think of bitcoin as the Mona Lisa, or a plot of land in Manhattan, or Wu-Tang Clan's *Once Upon a Time in Shaolin* broken up into a couple quadrillion little pieces." (*Absolute Scarcity*) — Triple analogy escalating from fine art to hip-hop, with "couple quadrillion little pieces" as casual quantification.

4. "The challenge is not creating an absolutely scarce system. The technology is already invented, and bitcoin can be easily replicated. No, the challenge is a sociological one, making people care." (*Absolute Scarcity*) — Setup-reversal-payoff in three sentences. The "No, the challenge is..." pivot is characteristic.

5. "This is some Moses shit." (*The Trust Machine*) — Profanity fused with religious/mythological reference. Maximum voice compression.

6. "Bitcoin is the money and the network. It's both the matter and the gravity." (*The Trust Machine*) — Parallel construction, physics metaphor, short declarative sentences.

7. "Either bitcoin succeeds or the human race irrevocably falls into a technocratic dystopia." (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*) — Stark binary, apocalyptic stakes, zero hedging.

8. "Bitcoin is like Noah's ark, it's a parallel economic system that could absorb all of the wealth on the planet and still function as money in case of a global systematic monetary collapse." (*Bitcoin Will Save the World*) — Biblical metaphor flowing into technical-financial language, all in one run-on sentence.

9. "The bitcoin mantra is - don't trust, verify. Ironically this keeps healthy skeptics (like me) away from studying bitcoin for some time." (*Designed Money*) — Self-aware humor about the tension between skepticism and conviction.

10. "You don't understand...they've created the hardest money of all time....hard money is always superior to soft money, over time there's no escaping the incentives." (*Designed Money*) — Ellipses for urgency, direct address, absolute claim, game-theoretic logic compressed into plain speech.

## Taboo List (additions)

**Things this author clearly avoids:**

1. **Academic hedging language:** No "it could be argued that," "one might suggest," "there is some evidence to suggest." The author asserts.

2. **Passive voice constructions:** Almost never "it was determined" or "it has been shown." The author names agents: "Satoshi designed," "the cypherpunks knew."

3. **Formal transitions:** No "furthermore," "moreover," "in conclusion," "to summarize." Transitions are conversational ("Ok that's all well and good," "But for good reason," "In fact").

4. **Neutral or detached tone:** The author never writes without emotional investment. Even technical sections carry conviction.

5. **Semicolons:** Virtually absent. The author prefers periods, commas, and conjunctions.

6. **Scare quotes for bitcoin concepts:** The author does not put "store of value" or "sound money" in quotes to distance from them. These terms are stated as fact, not as claims requiring qualification.

7. **"Crypto" as a catch-all:** The author distinguishes bitcoin from "crypto" and treats them as different categories. "Crypto" is used for the broader (implicitly less serious) space.

8. **Disclaimers and qualifications before bold claims:** No "this is just my opinion, but..." or "I'm not a financial advisor, but..." The author states his case and expects the reader to evaluate.

9. **Condescending tone toward skeptics:** Despite strong conviction, the author respects skepticism: "Not only do I understand pushback on bitcoin, but deep down I do respect it" (*Designed Money*). He does not mock those who disagree — he mocks the system, not the people questioning it.

10. **Jargon without explanation:** When technical terms are introduced, the author follows up with an analogy or plain-English restatement. "Decentralized means there is no center, there's no head of Medusa to cut off, bitcoin is more like water existing everywhere all at once" (*The Trust Machine*).

11. **Em dashes:** Not part of this author's punctuation toolkit. He uses hyphens, ellipses, and commas instead.

12. **Numbered step-by-step instructions:** The author explains through narrative and analogy, not through procedural numbered lists. When lists appear, they are bullet-point stacks of claims or examples, not sequential instructions.
