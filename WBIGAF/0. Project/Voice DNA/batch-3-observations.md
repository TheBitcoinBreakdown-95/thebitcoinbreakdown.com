## L1: Orthography & Visual Style

### Capitalization
- **"Bitcoin" vs "bitcoin"**: The author follows the community convention inconsistently but intentionally. Capital-B "Bitcoin" for the network/system, lowercase "bitcoin" for the currency/unit. However, in practice the author frequently uses capital-B even when referring to the money: "bitcoin is digital gold" vs "Bitcoin is immortal" — the capital form dominates in emphatic or proclamatory statements. (Evidence: what-is-bitcoin-3.md, orange-not-gold.md, why-should-you-care.md)
- **ALL CAPS rarely used** in body text. The author prefers *italics* and **bold** for emphasis over CAPS. Exception: "ACTUALLY" in about.md ("what bitcoin ACTUALLY was"), "BRAIN" in what-is-bitcoin-2.md ("from one BRAIN to another").
- **Section headings**: Mix of H1, H2, H3 without strict hierarchy. The author often repeats the same heading question multiple times in a single post as a structural device: "What is bitcoin?" appears 4 times in what-is-bitcoin.md, each time reframing the answer at a deeper level.

### Formatting habits
- **Italic text** used heavily for: emphasis on key phrases ("*a collection of many ideas*"), internal monologue or voice inflection ("*really* tough subject"), setting up dramatic moments ("*Let's turn back the clocks*"), and sarcastic or playful asides ("*almost* the most cataclysmic economic event").
- **Bold** used sparingly, mainly for key terms and the word "really" ("it's a **really** tough subject").
- **Horizontal rules** (asterisks or underscores) used to separate major sections, especially between quoted definitions.
- **Bullet lists**: The author creates lists when cataloguing Bitcoin analogies or properties, but body prose is paragraph-based. Lists are informal — often lack parallel grammatical structure.
- **Images**: Heavy use of AI-generated images between paragraphs to create visual breathing room. Images are decorative/atmospheric, not informational.
- **American English spelling** throughout: "favor," "color" implied (though these specific words rarely appear). Uses "realize" not "realise."

### Distinctive visual patterns
- One-word or two-word lines used as dramatic beats: "Vanished." / "Like a ghost." / "Is real." (orange-not-gold.md)
- Ellipses (...) and em-dashes (—) create visual pauses on the page.
- The author uses line breaks as white space to control pacing — many "paragraphs" are single sentences isolated for emphasis.

## L2: Punctuation & Micro-Prosody

### Ellipses (...)
- **Extremely frequent**. The author's most characteristic punctuation mark. Used for trailing off, dramatic pauses, building suspense, and conversational hesitation:
  - "...14 years later..." (orange-not-gold.md)
  - "...an irreversible curse was cast on humanity..." (orange-not-gold.md)
  - "...and it has spread to the point of no return." (orange-not-gold.md)
  - "cute nerd money that would just fade away someday..." (what-is-bitcoin.md)
  - "and yet...nothing comes anywhere close" (about.md)
  - "You're journey begins...with a simple question.." (what-is-bitcoin.md — note the double period variant)
- Ellipses serve as the author's primary tool for creating conversational rhythm in written text.

### Em dashes (—)
- Used for interjections and parenthetical asides: "trust me — I've spent over a decade on the internet" (about.md), "once I fell down the rabbit hole — I too became obsessed" (about.md), "far to few understand its significance" (about.md).
- Also used for dramatic pivot: "But...if you don't believe me, or don't get it yet, that's ok!" (about.md)

### Exclamation marks
- Used more than typical formal writing but not excessively. Concentrated in moments of genuine enthusiasm or incredulity: "And anyone can access it, no exceptions!" (what-is-bitcoin.md), "Creating technology that no government or corporation could stop!" (orange-not-gold.md), "Grab your popcorn, cus the next 20 years are going to be fucking wild!" (orange-not-gold.md)
- Almost never doubled (!! is rare). Single exclamation used as genuine emphasis, not hyperbole.

### Question marks
- Frequent use of rhetorical questions to structure arguments and engage the reader: "So why should you care at all?" (why-should-you-care.md), "But with its creator gone, what is to become of this digital money project? Does it fade away into obscurity?" (mystery-of-satoshi.md), "Is Satoshi an alien? A time traveler?" (mystery-of-satoshi.md)

### Commas
- The author uses commas liberally, sometimes where a period or semicolon would be more standard. Run-on tendencies are common, creating a breathless, conversational quality: "Someone who took fees, or took 7-10 business days, or who declined your transaction or froze your withdrawal." (other-peoples-bitcoin-definitions.md)

### Semicolons
- Almost entirely absent. The author avoids semicolons in favor of commas, dashes, or new sentences.

### Periods after fragments
- Frequent use of periods after sentence fragments for dramatic punch: "Like a ghost." / "Quite the opposite" / "Go figure." / "If you're curious."

## L4: Syntax & Sentence Geometry

### Sentence length
- Highly variable. The author alternates between long, comma-heavy sentences and very short punchy ones. The short sentences often follow long buildup passages to create emphasis:
  - Long: "Simultaneously reinventing one of the oldest technologies in human civilization, and solving a multi-thousand-year-old trust problem, with the first accounting innovation in over 700 years, Satoshi created a trust machine that runs on its own without human involvement." (mystery-of-satoshi.md)
  - Short: "Like a ghost." / "Is real." / "Go figure." / "If you're curious."

### Fragment usage
- **Extremely frequent.** This author uses fragments as one of their primary stylistic tools:
  - "A shadowy figure quietly emerges" (mystery-of-satoshi.md)
  - "A problem once thought impossible to solve." (orange-not-gold.md)
  - "Digital liberty advocates and anarchists." (orange-not-gold.md)
  - "Set loose into cyberspace!" (orange-not-gold.md)
  - "Infected, outwitted, and unseated." (orange-not-gold.md)
  - "If you're curious." (about.md)
  - "Go figure." (why-was-bitcoin-invented.md)
  - "No Leaders. No Rulers." (the-trust-machine-speech.md)

### Sentence starters
- **"It's"** — extremely common opener: "It's a tale as old as civilization itself," "It's the story of a cryptic computer scientist," "It's a question everyone asks," "It's a departure without a display," "It's estimated that Satoshi"
- **"And"** — used deliberately to start sentences for emphasis or continuation: "And independent money was their holy grail," "And their breakthrough in inventing bitcoin," "And it's so ubiquitous"
- **"But"** — also used at sentence starts frequently: "But with its creator gone," "But once you study it," "But that system is ripe for corruption"
- **"For you see"** — a distinctive opener used for revelatory moments: "For you see, this unknown inventor of bitcoin, was a Cypherpunk" (mystery-of-satoshi.md, orange-not-gold.md). This is an almost archaic, storytelling construction.
- **"Because"** — starts sentences as a causal connector in conversation: "Because this story, is real." (orange-not-gold.md)
- **"Imagine"** — used to launch thought experiments: "Imagine the most powerful tool of civilization, pried from the clutches of the elite" (about.md), "Imagine there's this huge elephant" (what-is-bitcoin-2.md)

### Active voice dominance
- The author overwhelmingly uses active voice. Passive constructions are rare and mainly appear in more technical or AI-influenced passages (the unparalleled posts).

### Parallel construction
- The author excels at building momentum through parallel structure, especially in lists of "can't" or "no":
  - "It can't be shut down, it can't be hacked, it can't be banned, it can't be changed, and it can't be controlled." (mystery-of-satoshi.md)
  - "No Vitalik Buterin, no Ethereum Foundation, no Deltec bank like Tether, no fancy offices in San Francisco, no team of lawyers, no governance token, no VC-backing, no pre-mine, no small council, and no whales" (unparalleled-new.md)
  - "No dictator, no king, no president, no CEO, no billionaire, no illuminati" (the-trust-machine-speech.md)
  - "No government, bank, corporation, secret society, international financial institution, oligarch, pope, dictator, or king" (what-is-bitcoin.md)

### Tricolon and escalation
- The author frequently builds in threes or escalating series:
  - "It's not about getting rich, and it's not about the price. It's about freedom. It's about monetary revolution. It's about global economic disruption." (about.md)
  - "Satoshi discovered a way to put physics into the internet...Satoshi discovered a new form of time...Satoshi discovered a way to give everyone on earth access to immutable money" (mystery-of-satoshi.md)

## L5: Cadence & Paragraph Choreography

### One-sentence paragraphs
- **Extremely frequent.** This is perhaps the most defining feature of the author's visual style. Many passages consist of a series of one-sentence paragraphs stacked for rhythmic effect:
  - "A shadowy figure quietly emerges" (standalone paragraph)
  - "They call them-self Satoshi Nakamoto" (standalone paragraph)
  - "Bitcoin is just getting started." (standalone paragraph)
  - "This is a Halloween story scarier than any other." then "Because this story," then "Is real." (three consecutive one-line paragraphs in orange-not-gold.md)

### Pacing pattern: build-up then punch
- The author's signature rhythm is a long, complex, information-dense sentence or paragraph followed by a very short, punchy line:
  - "For a couple of years, this mysterious computer scientist worked with volunteers to bootstrap the project. Fixing bugs and tweaking the code. And then suddenly...disappears. Like a ghost." (mystery-of-satoshi.md)
  - "Humans can't be trusted to maintain a currency. Throughout history we have succumb to the temptation of making money without working for it. / Go figure." (why-was-bitcoin-invented.md)

### Dramatic reveal structure
- The author builds suspense through fragmentation, especially in narrative posts:
  - In orange-not-gold.md, the actual word "bitcoin" is withheld for the first 10+ paragraphs, replaced by "this digital invention," "this malware network," "this computer cancer" — the reveal that it's bitcoin comes later as a pivot. This is a conscious rhetorical strategy.

### White space as breath
- The author uses blank lines liberally between short paragraphs, creating a vertical, airy feel. Posts read more like poetry or screenplay formatting than traditional blog prose.

### Paragraph length distribution
- Body paragraphs in explanatory mode: 3-6 sentences.
- Body paragraphs in proclamatory/narrative mode: 1-2 sentences.
- The author never writes dense, academic-style paragraphs (8+ sentences). The longest paragraphs appear in the what-is-bitcoin.md draft's technical explanation sections.

## L6: Discourse Strategy & Rhetorical Moves

### Opening strategies
- **In medias res / dramatic scene-setting**: "On a Halloween night, 15 years ago / An anonymous and mysterious programmer, posted the solution to a computer science problem." (orange-not-gold.md)
- **Provocative declaration**: "The truth is, bitcoin doesn't give a fuck about you." (why-should-you-care.md)
- **Direct address + imperative**: "Forget everything you know about bitcoin." (what-is-bitcoin.md)
- **Physical/interactive engagement**: "Ok to kick off this talk, I'm going to need everyone to take out some cash" (the-trust-machine-speech.md)
- **Time-travel framing**: "Let's pull back the timechain for a moment here..." (why-was-bitcoin-invented.md)
- The author never opens with dry exposition or topic sentences. Every opening is designed to hook.

### Closing strategies
- **Call to action / forward link**: "next post: [title]" links appear at the end of many posts
- **Dramatic one-liner or slogan**: "We are all Satoshi" (mystery-of-satoshi.md), "In Code We Trust" (the-trust-machine-speech.md)
- **Warning/promise about the future**: "Grab your popcorn, cus the next 20 years are going to be fucking wild!" (orange-not-gold.md), "shit's gonna get wild..." (about.md)
- **Pivot to bigger picture**: "But I'm getting a bit ahead of myself." (why-should-you-care.md)
- The author almost never summarizes. Endings are forward-looking or emotionally resonant, not recaps.

### Quote integration
- **Extremely quote-heavy**. The author frequently structures entire posts around others' definitions (other-peoples-bitcoin-definitions.md). Pattern: present quote in blockquote, then explain/translate in the author's own colloquial voice.
- The author treats quoted experts with genuine respect and deference: "by far the most accurate and comprehensive bitcoin explanation I've ever found" (other-peoples-bitcoin-definitions.md)
- Sources quoted most frequently: Andreas Antonopoulos, Gigi, Satoshi Nakamoto, Tomer Strolight, NYDIG.

### Rhetorical questions
- Used constantly to structure arguments and create the feeling of a dialogue with the reader:
  - "So why should you care at all?" (why-should-you-care.md)
  - "But with its creator gone, what is to become of this digital money project?" (mystery-of-satoshi.md)
  - "Is Satoshi an alien? A time traveler? A secret government organization? A libertarian nerd in his mother's basement?" (mystery-of-satoshi.md)
  - "You might be wondering, what's the point, why was bitcoin created? what's the end game?" (orange-not-gold.md)

### The "reframe" move
- The author frequently presents a simple/common understanding, then immediately reframes it as inadequate: "In simple terms bitcoin is digital money. But then again so are dollars, euros, and yen." (what-is-bitcoin.md) / "While digital money is technically correct! It doesn't really tell us anything..." (what-is-bitcoin.md) / "But still this definition of 'digital gold' is underwhelming." (what-is-bitcoin.md)

### The confessional/personal testimony move
- The author repeatedly inserts their own journey as evidence: "It took me a hundred hours to understand it," "I too was skeptical about bitcoin for many years," "I knew about bitcoin for a long time, but in hindsight I didn't know anything," "It took me 9 years of knowing about bitcoin...before I realized." This personal testimony is a core persuasive strategy — the author positions their conversion narrative as proof.

### The pronoun shift: "her/his/they" for Satoshi
- A deliberate and distinctive move: the author alternates pronouns for Satoshi within the same post and sometimes within the same paragraph: "no one ever hears her voice and no one ever sees his face" (mystery-of-satoshi.md), "No one even knows if he is dead or alive, and yet, this invention has made her worth of over $30 billion" (orange-not-gold.md). This is clearly intentional — emphasizing that Satoshi's identity is unknown and could be anyone.

### The "imagine" invitation
- A recurring move where the author asks the reader to envision something: "Imagine the most powerful tool of civilization, pried from the clutches of the elite." "Imagine there's this huge elephant." "Imagine creating an unstoppable technology that uses game theory to hijack human greed." This creates participatory engagement.

## L7: Epistemic Posture & Thinking Style

### Confidence level
- **Very high conviction** about Bitcoin's importance and inevitability. The author uses absolute language: "bitcoin is completely inevitable," "I've never been so sure about something in my entire life," "Bitcoin is the best money in history," "the greatest monetary asset ever developed."
- No hedging on core Bitcoin thesis. The author never says "maybe Bitcoin is important" — it's always presented as certain.
- Slight hedging only on specific future predictions: "It's predicted that this treasure surpasses a trillion dollars in value as soon as the 2030's" (mystery-of-satoshi.md), "Will fiat debasement and bitcoin adoption lead to increased purchasing power? Maybe." (about.md)

### Authority signaling
- The author signals authority through **time invested**, not credentials: "hundreds of hours," "over a decade on the internet," "9 years of knowing about bitcoin," "years of research and internal debate." The implicit argument: "I've put in the work, so trust my conclusion."
- The author also signals authority through **past skepticism**: repeatedly mentioning that they were once a skeptic serves to pre-empt the reader's skepticism. "I too was skeptical," "I didn't believe bitcoiners for a very long time."

### Relationship to reader
- **Guide/mentor, not lecturer.** The author positions themselves as someone who has been through the journey and is now leading others: "My job is to make your fall down the rabbit hole as easy as possible," "My job of course is to lead you little monks all around the elephant."
- **Fellow human, not expert.** The author doesn't claim technical expertise or academic authority. They claim experiential authority — having been converted themselves.
- **Occasional confrontational/challenging mode**: "I personally don't think you should be allowed an opinion on bitcoin if you can't explain what it is" (why-should-you-care.md), "If you're still ignoring bitcoin or calling it a bubble, maybe it's finally time to ask why you've been proven wrong again and again" (why-should-you-care.md)
- **Protective/caring undertone**: "I'm here to warn you" (about.md), "I honestly feel bad for people who don't understand yet" (why-will-bitcoin-win.md)

### Thinking style
- **Synthesis over analysis**: The author is more interested in making connections and building a holistic picture than in drilling into technical details. Technical concepts are always translated to plain-language analogies.
- **Narrative-first**: Arguments are structured as stories, not logical proofs. Even when presenting technical information, it's wrapped in narrative framing (Satoshi's story, the cypherpunks' mission, the author's own journey).
- **Maximalist**: The author is a Bitcoin maximalist and does not hide it. Other cryptocurrencies are "straight up scams" or "not trying to compete." The scope of claims is always maximal: "changed the course of human civilization," "the most important invention in economics and technology since the internet."
- **Moral framing**: Bitcoin is consistently framed as a moral/ethical project, not just a technological one: "fighting back against authoritarianism," "protects human rights for 8 billion people," "the separation of money and state." The author sees Bitcoin through a justice/freedom lens.
