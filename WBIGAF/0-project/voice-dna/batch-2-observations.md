# Batch 2 Observations — "Bitcoin is X" Series

**Posts analyzed:** 8
1. Bitcoin is Money
2. Bitcoin is Digital Gold
3. Bitcoin is Time
4. Bitcoin is Information
5. Bitcoin is the Internet of Money
6. Bitcoin is a Network
7. Bitcoin is Ethical Money
8. The Great Excel Spreadsheet in the Sky

**Total word count (approx):** ~5,800 words across 8 posts (several are short-form explainers; "Internet of Money" is the longest at ~1,600 words)

---

## L1: Orthography & Visual Style

**Bitcoin/bitcoin capitalization:** The author follows a deliberate convention — "Bitcoin" (capital B) for the network/system/protocol, "bitcoin" (lowercase) for the currency/unit. This is applied consistently across the series, e.g. "bitcoin the currency, is sent over Bitcoin the network, by computers running Bitcoin the software" (Bitcoin is a Network). However, the author is not rigid about it — within flowing prose the distinction sometimes blurs, especially mid-sentence.

**American English spelling:** Consistent American spellings throughout. No British conventions observed.

**Bold and italic usage:** Bold is rare — used almost exclusively inside blockquotes or for defining terms in context. Italic is used more freely for emphasis and for quasi-definitional statements. In "Bitcoin is Digital Gold," the opening definition section is entirely italicized, using it as a visual marker that says "these are the headline definitions":
- *"Bitcoin is the first and most popular cryptocurrency!"*
- *"It is the invention of digital scarcity i.e. digital property."*
- *"It's a new form of money and an independent global monetary network."*

**ALL CAPS:** Used sparingly but deliberately for emphasis. "Money HAS to be information" (Bitcoin is Money). Not a frequent device — the author prefers other emphasis methods.

**Parenthetical asides:** Very common. The author uses parentheses to insert casual commentary, caveats, or humor mid-thought:
- "(Which by the way, is why there was often a bi-metallic standard where silver fulfilled some of the divisibility and portability shortcomings with gold.)" (Bitcoin is Money)
- "(Instead of rapidly destroying the global economy, with bitcoin endogenously reborn from fiat ashes)" (Internet of Money)

**Section headings:** Uses `##` headings frequently. Headings are often declarative statements ("Bitcoin is Digital Commodity Money," "Bitcoin is Designed Money") or questions ("So, what exactly is Bitcoin?", "Why is bitcoin the internet of money and not the larger crypto ecosystem?"). Some headings are intentionally colloquial.

**Abbreviations:** "I.e." and "i.e." used frequently — sometimes correctly, sometimes loosely. "Etc." also common. These give the writing a textbook-meets-casual feel.

**Minor orthographic quirks:**
- "it's" sometimes used where "its" is correct: "it's periphery" (Internet of Money), "it's price listed" (Bitcoin is Money). This is a consistent error, not a style choice.
- "Refrences" misspelled in at least two posts (Bitcoin is Time, Internet of Money).
- "wont" without apostrophe (Internet of Money).
- Occasional comma splices and run-ons treated as acceptable prose.

## L2: Punctuation & Micro-Prosody

**Ellipses (...):** A signature punctuation device. Used frequently for trailing-off, building anticipation, or softening transitions:
- "So while digital money is technically correct! It doesn't really tell us anything..." (Digital Gold)
- "Multi party custody solutions, payment channels, atomic swaps, batch transactions, time locks, vaults, prisms, micropayments...and so much more that hasn't even been invented yet." (Information)
- "why BTC and not ETH, SOL, ADA, ALGO, TRX, MATIC, ATOM, AVAX....." (Internet of Money — note the five dots, exaggerated ellipsis)

**Em dashes (—) vs hyphens (-):** The author almost never uses proper em dashes. Instead, uses double hyphens (--) occasionally: "you could create social relationships, transactions and trade-- you could feed yourself" (quote in Bitcoin is Money). More commonly uses commas, ellipses, or parentheses where a dash might go. This is a notable absence.

**Exclamation marks:** Used sparingly but with purpose — often to inject enthusiasm or mark a surprising statement:
- "Money was so important in society we had to invent writing to keep track of it!" (Bitcoin is Money)
- "So while digital money is technically correct!" (Digital Gold)
- "You can send someone money with a meme!" (Information)

**Question marks and rhetorical questions:** Frequent. The author uses questions as structural pivots — to transition between sections, to set up the next argument, or to voice the reader's skepticism:
- "Why then did this ancient money become obsolete?" (Digital Gold)
- "The real question is, why do people believe in certain monies over other ones?" (Bitcoin is Money)
- "If gold was independently chosen by world civilizations for thousands of years due to its superior monetary properties, what does that mean for digital gold?" (Digital Gold)

**Commas:** The author uses commas generously but inconsistently. Oxford comma is sometimes present, sometimes absent. Comma splices are common and appear intentional — they create a rushing, breathless quality:
- "Bitcoin is a global interconnected computer network made up of tens of thousands of nodes." (Network — clean)
- "You can think of Bitcoin as a kind of clock, it keeps track of time across the tens of thousands of computers" (Time — comma splice)

**Periods after short punchy statements:** The author frequently deploys very short sentences terminated with a period for emphasis. These function as verbal mic-drops:
- "And anyone can access it, no exceptions." (Spreadsheet)
- "And once a bitcoin transaction is sent it can't be undone." (Information)
- "Ticking away for eternity." (Time)

## L3: Lexicon & Collocations

**Signature phrases and recurring collocations:**
- "at the speed of light" — appears in at least 3 posts (Digital Gold, Information, Spreadsheet). A favorite metaphor for bitcoin's transfer speed.
- "who sent money to who" — used verbatim in multiple posts (Spreadsheet uses it twice, Network once). Characteristic phrasing.
- "without permission" / "permissionless" — recurring across posts (Information, Internet of Money). Core ideological vocabulary.
- "just a ledger" / "just a spreadsheet" — the reductive simplification. Used in Spreadsheet ("That's literally all bitcoin is, it's just a ledger") and Information ("bitcoin is just an internet ledger").
- "scratching the surface" — used twice in Internet of Money ("money is just scratching the surface," "just scratching the surface though").
- "can't be shut down" / "unstoppable" — appears in Time ("can't be shut off") and Spreadsheet ("can't be hacked or shut down").
- "the best [X] ever [made/invented]" — "best money ever made" (Bitcoin is Money), "best savings technology ever invented" (Ethical Money).
- "sound money" — appears repeatedly (Digital Gold, Bitcoin is Money, Internet of Money). Core term used without hedging.
- "from the ground up" — "Designed from the ground up" (Bitcoin is Money).

**Vocabulary register:** Deliberately accessible. The author avoids jargon when possible and when jargon is necessary, immediately explains it:
- "The fancy term for this is Bitcoin is a distributed timestamp server" (Time)
- "Specifically, bitcoin is a peer-to-peer network, where every computer can communicate with any other computer directly." (Network)

When technical terms do appear, they're often immediately followed by plain-language restatements.

**Casual/conversational words:**
- "heck" — "heck you can even send money over Facebook messenger!" (Digital Gold)
- "cool" — "The cool thing about digital property though" (Bitcoin is Money)
- "honestly" — "honestly I do like it a lot better" (Digital Gold)
- "crazy" — "The crazy thing about bitcoin is though" (Digital Gold)
- "kind of like" — "kind of like a big mushroom" (Network)
- "if you will" — "A decentralized clock if you will" (Time), "A spreadsheet if you will" (Bitcoin is Money)
- "So to recap:" (Network)

**Analogy and metaphor vocabulary:**
- Mushroom/blob for decentralized network (Network)
- Clock/ticking for time-keeping function (Time)

- "beaming digital super-gold around the world" (Spreadsheet)

**Profanity:** None observed in this batch. The tone stays clean.

**Pop culture / real-world references:** PayPal, Venmo, Cash App, Facebook Messenger, Google Docs, Excel, AOL, Myspace, Kohls cash (Digital Gold). These ground abstract concepts in everyday experience.

**Favorite intensifiers:** "truly" ("truly boundless," "truly revolutionary"), "extremely" ("extremely portable," "extremely secure," "extremely simplified"), "completely" (used four times in one sentence in Ethical Money: "completely global, completely borderless, completely decentralized, completely open").

## L4: Syntax & Sentence Geometry

**Sentence starters — And/But/So/Or:** Very frequent. The author regularly starts sentences with coordinating conjunctions, giving the writing a conversational, forward-moving momentum:
- "And it's so ubiquitous that many of us take it for granted." (Bitcoin is Money)
- "But then again - so are dollars, euros, and yen." (Digital Gold)
- "So while digital money is technically correct!" (Digital Gold)
- "And to confuse you even more..." (Network)
- "And anyone can access it, no exceptions." (Spreadsheet)
- "And once a bitcoin transaction is sent it can't be undone." (Information)

**Fragments for punch:** Used deliberately as emphatic closers:
- "Ticking away for eternity." (Time)
- "A spreadsheet if you will, like a bank account, clay tablet, or credit card." (Bitcoin is Money)
- "Based on rules not rulers." (Internet of Money)
- "The separation of money and state." (Internet of Money)

**Parallel constructions and lists:** A dominant syntactic pattern. The author loves building lists of properties, features, or comparisons — both in comma-separated runs and in parallel sentence structures:
- "It's sound money, it's unstoppable money, it's portable, verifiable, divisible, durable, fungible, and easily verifiable money." (Bitcoin is Money)
- "bitcoin is borderless, permission-less, peer-to-peer, resistant to censorship, pseudonymous, extremely secure, and settles with cash finality." (Bitcoin is Money)
- "The internet is programmable information... Bitcoin is programmable money... The internet is decentralized informational infrastructure, bitcoin is decentralized monetary infrastructure." (Internet of Money)

**"You can..." constructions:** The author frequently uses "you can" to invite the reader into the possibility space:
- "You can send a bitcoin transaction via QR code...or in a picture." (Information)
- "You can text a bitcoin transaction with emojis, you can send it via satellite, you can send it over radio" (Information)
- "You can think of the bitcoin network kind of like a big mushroom" (Network)
- "you can think of it like an ultimate google doc" (Spreadsheet)

**Active voice dominance:** The writing is overwhelmingly active voice. Passive constructions are rare and usually appear only in technical definitions or quotes.

**Sentence length:** Highly variable. Short punchy sentences alternate with long, comma-heavy runs. Some sentences stack multiple clauses:
- "Multi party custody solutions, payment channels, atomic swaps, batch transactions, time locks, vaults, prisms, micropayments, in-game currency integration, money streaming, insurance and options smart contracts, layer three web browsers, social networks, encrypted messaging.... and so much more that hasn't even been invented yet." (Information — 39 words in a breathless list)

**"This is because..." / "The reason is...":** A characteristic explanatory construction. The author frequently uses explicit causal connectors:
- "This is because gold just so happens to fulfill certain monetary properties" (Digital Gold)
- "This is because even though gold has served as money" (Bitcoin is Money)
- "The reason this is so revolutionary is because" (Internet of Money)
- "The reason why this is good is because" (Internet of Money)

## L5: Cadence & Paragraph Choreography

**One-sentence paragraphs:** Extremely frequent and a defining feature of the voice. Used for emphasis, transitions, and punchy standalone statements:
- "And anyone can access it, no exceptions." (Spreadsheet)
- "And once a bitcoin transaction is sent it can't be undone." (Information)
- "It exists as permanently and beyond human control as time itself." (Time)
- "Bitcoin democratizes value in the same way that the internet democratizes information." (Internet of Money)
- "Everyone intuitively understands that it's always about the money. Always has been." (Bitcoin is Money — two-sentence paragraph functioning as a one-liner)

**Build-up then punch pattern:** The author consistently builds context with longer paragraphs, then delivers a short emphatic conclusion:
- Long paragraph explaining how gold certificates evolved → "Eventually this system broke down and only unbacked paper/digital money was left." (Digital Gold)
- Long paragraph about bitcoin's monetary properties → "Bitcoin isn't just money, it's the reinvention of money itself." (Bitcoin is Money)

**Paragraph length:** Most paragraphs are 2-4 sentences. Rarely exceeds 5 sentences. The author breaks frequently to maintain rhythm and readability. Dense technical paragraphs (like the bitcoin tech stack list in Internet of Money) are immediately followed by short, accessible paragraphs.

**White space as rhythm:** The author uses frequent paragraph breaks to create breathing room. Technical content is broken into small digestible chunks rather than dense blocks. This creates a scrolling, discovery-oriented reading experience.

**Section transitions:** Typically handled by rhetorical questions or direct address ("If you think about it from an adversarial perspective," "In order for you to understand what bitcoin is, you unfortunately have to understand what the internet is").

## L6: Discourse Strategy & Rhetorical Moves

**Opening strategies:** Two main patterns observed:
1. **Bold definitional statement:** Jump straight into a claim or definition — "Money is just the base layer of an economic system" (Internet of Money), "Bitcoin is a global interconnected computer network" (Network), "You can think of Bitcoin as a kind of clock" (Time).
2. **Question or challenge:** "So, what exactly is Bitcoin?" (Digital Gold), "In order to understand what bitcoin is, you have to understand what money is" (Bitcoin is Money).

The author never opens with an anecdote or personal story in this batch. Openings are always conceptual.

**Closing strategies:** Two patterns:
1. **Grand aspirational statement:** "We're building revolutionary systems that will last generations." (Internet of Money), "Bitcoin isn't just money, it's the reinvention of money itself." (Bitcoin is Money).
2. **Navigation link to next post:** Every post ends with a "[next post: ...]" link. The series is designed as a sequential journey.

**Analogy as primary teaching tool:** The author's dominant rhetorical strategy is analogy. Nearly every abstract concept is grounded in a familiar comparison:
- Bitcoin as clock (Time)
- Bitcoin as Google Doc / Excel spreadsheet (Spreadsheet)
- Bitcoin as gold on the internet (Digital Gold)
- Bitcoin as unstoppable PayPal (Spreadsheet)
- Bitcoin network as mushroom/brain (Network)
- Bitcoin as internet but for money (Internet of Money)
- Email = internet mail, bitcoin = gold coins on the internet (Digital Gold)

**Expert quote integration:** The author leans heavily on Andreas Antonopoulos, quoting him in at least 3 of the 8 posts (Bitcoin is Money, Digital Gold, Internet of Money). Also quotes Jack Mallers, Gregory Trubetskoy, and "Mastering Bitcoin." Quotes are used to add authority and to express ideas the author endorses but wants to attribute. The author often introduces quotes casually: "To steal another line from Andreas Antonopoulos:" (Digital Gold).

**Reductio / simplification move:** A recurring strategy — reduce bitcoin to its simplest possible description, then expand outward:
- "That's literally all bitcoin is, it's just a ledger" (Spreadsheet)
- "It's technically just 1's and 0's computer code, and cool math!" (Information)
- Then immediately escalate: "Except of course the bitcoin ledger is the most powerful and secure supercomputer on earth" (Spreadsheet)

**Counterargument acknowledgment:** The author anticipates objections and addresses them directly:
- "The obvious reaction to this of course is, bitcoin is just one blockchain network among an entire crypto ecosystem" (Internet of Money)
- "So when people say, bitcoin isn't real, it's the same as saying your money in the bank isn't real." (Spreadsheet)
- "As a pragmatist I would concede that the likelihood of full market share of the global economy within my lifetime is slim" (Internet of Money)

**"If you think about it..." / invitation to perspective-shift:** The author frequently nudges the reader to adopt a new mental frame:
- "If you think about it from an adversarial perspective" (Information)
- "If gold was independently chosen by world civilizations for thousands of years...what does that mean for digital gold?" (Digital Gold)

**Escalation pattern:** Start with a simple claim, then escalate to a grander one:
- "Not only does bitcoin perfectly fit all of the traditional properties of good money, AND vastly improve on digital fiat, but bitcoin also has additional properties that were never before possible." (Bitcoin is Money — triple escalation: fits, improves, transcends)

## L7: Epistemic Posture & Thinking Style

**Confidence level: High.** The author writes with conviction. Claims are presented as settled facts, not hypotheses:
- "bitcoin was designed to be superior to all other forms of money" (Bitcoin is Money)
- "bitcoin is the undisputed champion of sound money" (Digital Gold)
- "Bitcoin is the best savings technology ever invented" (Ethical Money)

**Hedging — rare but present for future predictions:** The author hedges primarily when making forward-looking claims:
- "As a pragmatist I would concede that the likelihood of full market share of the global economy within my lifetime is slim and might not happen for a hundred years." (Internet of Money)
- "the bitcoin onboarding process (should) and will take time" — note the parenthetical "(should)" which shows real-time self-correction (Internet of Money)

**Modal verbs:** "Can" is dominant — used to describe bitcoin's capabilities. "Will" is used for predictions about bitcoin's future. "Must" is almost never used. This reflects an author who describes possibilities rather than issuing commands.

**Reader relationship — teacher/guide:** The author positions themselves as an informed guide walking the reader through complex material:
- "In order for you to understand what bitcoin is, you unfortunately have to understand what the internet is." (Internet of Money)
- "If I was explaining bitcoin to a 5 year old" (Digital Gold)
- "And to confuse you even more" (Network) — acknowledges the reader's experience
- "If this is all still not making perfect sense to you" (Internet of Money) — empathetic check-in

**Personal disclosure — rare but impactful:** The author occasionally inserts personal perspective, always to signal authenticity:
- "this is the key insight that flipped the switch for me personally and made me realize what bitcoin actually is" (Internet of Money)
- "honestly I do like it a lot better" (Digital Gold)
- "As a pragmatist I would concede" (Internet of Money)

**Epistemic escalation:** The author often starts with a simple framing, then reveals that the simple framing is actually inadequate — pushing the reader toward a deeper understanding:
- "digital gold...is underwhelming. It is extremely simplified and doesn't really explain why bitcoin is so revolutionary." (Digital Gold)
- "The bitcoin stack is much more than just a money protocol, money is just scratching the surface." (Internet of Money)
- This creates a "you thought X, but actually Y" dynamic that propels the reader forward through the series.

**Authority signaling:** Done through quotes (Antonopoulos, Mallers), technical terminology fluency, and structural framing (the series format implies comprehensive knowledge). The author does not credential-signal or reference their own background.

## Taboo List (additions)

**Things this author clearly avoids:**

1. **Academic/formal register:** No footnotes, no formal citations (uses casual attribution like "To steal another line from Andreas Antonopoulos"), no hedged academic language ("it could be argued that...").

2. **First-person storytelling/anecdotes:** The author almost never tells personal stories. When "I" appears, it's brief and functional ("honestly I do like it a lot better," "the key insight that flipped the switch for me"). No "let me tell you about the time I..."

3. **Profanity and vulgarity:** Completely absent. The strongest language is "heck."

4. **Price talk and financial advice:** No price predictions, no "buy now" language, no portfolio allocation discussion. This is ideological and technical, not financial.

5. **Cynicism or defeatism:** The tone is always optimistic about bitcoin's future. Even when acknowledging challenges ("the entrenched fiat capitalist system...probably wont go away anytime soon"), the framing is hopeful.

6. **Condescension:** Despite writing for beginners, the author never talks down. Uses phrases like "If this is all still not making perfect sense to you" rather than "if you don't get it."

7. **Em dashes:** Nearly absent. The author uses commas, ellipses, parentheses, and sentence breaks instead.

8. **Passive voice:** Almost entirely avoided.

9. **Complex sentence subordination:** The author prefers coordination (and, but, so) over subordination (although, whereas, notwithstanding). Sentences chain forward rather than nest inward.

10. **Bullet-point-heavy structure:** Even when listing, the author prefers comma-separated inline lists over formatted bullet points. The Ethical Money post is a notable exception — and it reads like an outline/draft, not the author's polished style.

11. **Scare quotes for mockery:** The author uses quotation marks for actual quoting and defining terms, not for ironic distancing.

12. **Disclaimers and legal hedging:** No "this is not financial advice" boilerplate. No "in my opinion" qualifiers on factual claims.
