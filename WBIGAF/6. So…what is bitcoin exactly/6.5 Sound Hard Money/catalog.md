### Ludwig von Mises on Sound Money

16. Ludwig von Mises argues that sound money is an instrument for the protection of civil liberties and a means of limiting government power (1912). (Ludwig von Mises)
17. "It is impossible to grasp the meaning of the idea of sound money if one does not realize that it was devised as an instrument for the protection of civil liberties against despotic inroads on the part of governments." (Ludwig von Mises, *The Theory of Money and Credit*, 1912)
18. Sound money ideologically belongs in the same class as political constitutions and bills of rights. (Ludwig von Mises)
19. The demand for sound money was first a response to the princely practice of debasing the coinage. (Ludwig von Mises)
20. The sound-money principle has two aspects: affirmative (approving the market's choice of a medium of exchange) and negative (obstructing the government's propensity to meddle with the currency system). (Ludwig von Mises)
21. Sound money meant a metallic standard -- standard coins should be a definite quantity of the standard metal as precisely determined by law. (Ludwig von Mises)
22. The defeat of silver and the unfeasibility of bimetalism eventually made the sound-money principle mean the gold standard. (Ludwig von Mises)
23. At the end of the 19th century there was unanimity among businessmen and statesmen worldwide about the indispensability of the gold standard. (Ludwig von Mises)
24. "A sound doctrine can win only by exploding the delusions of its adversaries." (Ludwig von Mises)
25. "The excellence of the gold standard is to be seen in the fact that it renders the determination of the monetary unit's purchasing power independent of the policies of governments and political parties." (Ludwig von Mises)
26. The gold standard is an indispensable implement of constitutional government -- parliamentary control of finances only works if the government cannot provide for unauthorized expenditures by increasing fiat money. (Ludwig von Mises)

### Deflation Does Not Cause Depression -- Evidence

28. There is no social benefit from preventing price deflation. (Author, citing Atkinson & Kehoe)
29. UCLA economist Andrew Atkinson and Minneapolis Fed economist Patrick Kehoe (2004, *American Economic Review*): "A broad historical look finds more periods of deflation with reasonable growth than with depression, and many more periods of depression with inflation than with deflation." (Atkinson & Kehoe)
30. Looking at 17 countries over more than 100 years: "Overall, the data show virtually no link between deflation and depression." (Atkinson & Kehoe)
31. Even for the Great Depression, of 16 countries that experienced deflation, only 8 had a depression -- and the relationship was not statistically significant. (Atkinson & Kehoe)
32. For all other periods beginning in 1820: 65 of 73 deflation episodes had no depression and 21 of 29 depressions had no deflation. (Atkinson & Kehoe)
33. "In a broader historical context, beyond the Great Depression, the notion that deflation and depression are linked virtually disappears." (Atkinson & Kehoe)
34. A 1-percentage-point drop in inflation is associated with a drop in average real growth of just 0.08 of a percentage point -- a negligible effect. (Atkinson & Kehoe)
35. Pre-WW II data shows a stronger correlation between deflation and depression, but post-WW II data shows a correlation between inflation and depression. (Atkinson & Kehoe)
36. The monetary inflation and credit expansion of an elastic currency system would be eliminated along with the booms and busts that have plagued history. (Author)
