The core of the matter is that #bitcoin you have on an exchange isn’t actually yours.  
It’s a note from the exchange that says they owe you (“I owe you” aka IOU 😉) that amount of BTC.  
In practice, this means you depend on them honoring that debt so you can get your BTC back.  
History tells us exchanges aren’t really good in honoring that claim.  
So odds are you’ll not be able to claim your #bitcoin whenever you need/want them.  
Most of the time it might work, but one single event is enough to do lasting damage  
While all exchanges vow to take good care of customer funds, only a handful actually do.  
You can spend a lot of time and effort in trying to spot those OR spend that time in learning self custody.  
And it goes without saying - nothing prevents even “good” actors from failing.  
So, if you plan on owning #bitcoin or crypto for the medium or long term, it is undoubtedly worth it to face the learning curve and take your funds under your control.  
Ultimately, it’s a tradeoff between convenience and security, and only you can know what’s right for you.

Don't make this rookie mistake!  
The best way to hold your bitcoin is in your own wallet. This way you’ll never have to worry that you actually have less than you’re being told by a bank, exchange or other service provider. Don’t ask for permission to spend your own money. Hold your own bitcoin.  
It’s important that you don’t leave your satoshis lying around in your Cash App account. To ensure that you truly own your bitcoin, you should send them to a wallet that only you can access.
