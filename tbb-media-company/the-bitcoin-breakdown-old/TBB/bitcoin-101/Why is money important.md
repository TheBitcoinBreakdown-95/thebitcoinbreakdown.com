No means to evaluate success or failure and no motivation for such risk-transferring behavior.

Money is not just a convenience, but a necessity for the growth and development of human civilization. Without money, societies would be limited in their ability to expand beyond a certain size, known as Dunbar's number. This number, which is estimated to be around 150 people, represents the maximum number of individuals that a person can maintain social relationships with.  
Once a society surpasses this number, it becomes increasingly difficult to keep track of debts and credits, making complex exchange transactions nearly impossible. The web of connections just becomes too great, and society can't scale.  
This is where money comes in.  
Money acts as one of the most important tools for the development and sustainability of human civilization. Money provides a standardized and efficient means of exchange, facilitating complex transactions and enabling the specialization of labor.  
The only time where money becomes unnecessary is when people can perfectly trust one another. Credit relationships where you know they'll provide you an equal favor someday in return. In reality, this level of trust is difficult to achieve, especially in larger societies where people do not know each other well.  
Therefore, in larger more developed economies, money acts as a trust replacement technology.
