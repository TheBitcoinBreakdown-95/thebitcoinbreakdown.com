Chapter 2:

- Identity based habits
    - Need to start with changing what you believe, not what your system is or what your goals are
    - "The ultimate form of intrinsic motivation is when a habit becomes part of your identity"
        - **"The more pride you have in a particular aspect of your identity, the more motivated you will be to maintain the habits associated with it."**

Chapter 3:

- How to build better habits in 4 simple steps
    - A habit is a behavior that has been repeated enough times to become automatic
    - Habits are mental shortcuts
    - Habits do not restrict freedom, they create it
        - (Although I would argue doing the same thing every day is boring)
    - Cue, craving, response, reward
        - The cue triggers your brain to initiate a behavior
            - Your mind is constantly analyzing your environment for hits of where rewards are located
        - Cravings are the motivation for any habit/action
            - They are different for everyone
        - Response, or the actual habit you preform
            - If an action requires more physical or mental effort than you are willing to expend then you won't do it.
        - Rewards are the end goal of every habit
            - Your brain is a reward detector
    - If a behavior is insufficient in any of the four stages, it will not become a habit
- The four laws of behavior change
    - Turning these four steps into a practical framework that we can use to design good habits and eliminate bad ones
        - Cue - make it obvious
        - Craving - make it attractive
        - Response - make it easy
        - Reward - make it satisfying
    - Breaking bad habits
        - Cue - make it invisible
        - Craving - make it unattractive
        - Response - make it difficult
        - Reward - make it unsatisfying

- The cue is about noticing the reward
- The craving is about wanting the reward
- The response is about obtaining the reward
 
THE FIRST LAW: MAKE IT OBVIOUS

Chapter 5:

- The best way to start a new habit
    - Implementation intention - a plan you make beforehand about when and where to act.
        - Time and location are important cues
        - When situation X arises, I will perform response Y
    - Many people think they lack motivation, when what they really lack is clarity. Some people spend their entire lives waiting for the time to be right to make an improvement
        - We often say yes to little requests because we are not clear enough about what we need to be doing instead
    - Habit stacking
        - Each action becomes a cue that triggers the next behavior, no behavior happens in isolation
        - One of the best ways to build a new habit is to identify a current habit you already do each day and then stack your new behavior on top
        - Consider when you are most likely to be successful, don't ask yourself to do a habit when you're likely to be occupied with something else
          
        
 
Chapter 6:

- Motivation is Overrated: Environment often matters more
    - The story about putting water bottles everywhere and then people drank more water vs soda
        - Many of the actions we take each day are shaped not by purposeful drive and choice, but by the most obvious option
        - You don't have to be the victim of your environment, you can also be the architect of it
    - How to design your environment for success:
        - If you want to make a habit a big part of your life, make the cue a big part of your environment
            - The most persistent behaviors usually have multiple cues
            - By sprinkling triggers throughout your surroundings, you increase the odds you'll think about your habit throughout the day
        - One space one use
            - For the same reason that you shouldn't have your office in your bedroom, you should separate your spaces for each habit. A chair for meditating, a table for eating, and your phone only for texting and social media (vs having an ipod) by separating your spaces you set yourself up for an easier time keeping habits
            - A stable environment where everything has a place and a purpose is an environment where habits can easily form

Chapter 7:

- The secret to self-control
    - The story of heroin addict Vietnam vets returning home, and 90% of them quitting overnight
        - Disciplined people are better at structuring their lives in a way that does not require heroic willpower and self-control.
    - You can break a habit, but you are unlikely to forget it
        - Once the mental grooves of habit have been carved into your brain, they are nearly impossible to remove entirely
            - Simply resisting temptation is an ineffective strategy
            - You can't stick to positive habits in a negative environment.
        - The most reliable way to cut bad habits is at the source
            - Reduce exposure to the cue that causes it
            - Make it invisible
              
            
   

Chapter 8:

- How to make a habit irresistible
    - Starts with the story of the gulls pecking at the red dots, because it was instinct
        - Humans also react to stimuli, or exaggerated versions of reality, like food, and porn and social media dopamine spikes
    - If you want to increase the odds that a behavior will occur, then you need to make it attractive
        - Dopamine driven feedback loop
            - Habits are a dopamine driven feedback loop, every habit that is formed is associated with higher levels of dopamine.
            - *Key point, dopamine is released not only when you experience pleasure but when you anticipate it
                - This is what motivates us to action
                - It is the expectation of a rewarding experience that motivates us to act in the first place
    - How to use Temptation Bundling to make you habits more attractive
        - Temptation bundling works by linking an action you want to do with an action you need to do
 
Chapter 9:

- The Role of Family and Friends in Shaping Your Habits
    - Starts off with the story of the Hungarian man Lazlo Polgar who believed that anyone can become great, you don't need to be born with talent. He raised his 3 daughters to eat live and breathe chess and they all became protégés
        - I might want to do this with my kids and public speaking, it’s a tradeoff for being a jack of all trades
    - We don't choose our earliest habits, we imitate them
        - Social norms are the invisible rules that guide your behaviors
        - We all want to fit in
    - We imitate the habits of three groups:
        - The close
            - We pickup habits from people around us, for better or for worse
            - Surround yourself by people who have habits you want to have yourself
            - Nothing sustains motivation better than belonging to the tribe, you go from I am a reader to we are readers. Friendship and community embed new identities and help behaviors last over the long run
        - The many
            - When we are unsure of ow to act, we look to the group to guide our behavior, we are constantly scanning the room and wondering what is everyone else doing?
            - When changing your habits means fitting in with the tribe, change is attractive
        - The powerful
            - If a behavior can get us approval, respect, and praise, we find it attractive
