<summary><span class="section-label"><span class="block-num">01</span> The Oldest Trick in Civilization</span></summary>

<details open>
<summary><span class="section-label">Thesis</span></summary>
<div class="block-thesis">
Debasement is not a modern problem -- it is a human nature problem as old as organized society itself, and it always ends the same way.
</div>
</details>

<details open>
<summary><span class="section-label">Argument</span></summary>

1. As long as there have been people "making" money, there have been people making money *for themselves*
2. Rome: the denarius started at >90% silver, was diluted over 300 years down to 2%
3. Every ruler had the same justification -- wars, public works, loyalty payments -- and the same result: wealth transfer from citizens to the state
4. The percentage of silver in the denarius mirrors the stability of Rome overall -- <span class="glitch-hover" data-text="debasement">debasement</span> tracked the empire's decline
5. The pattern repeats across civilizations: need money → debase → short-term stability → long-term collapse
6. <span class="glitch-hover" data-text="Bitcoin">Bitcoin</span> solves this by making debasement impossible -- <span class="glitch-hover" data-text="21 million">21 million</span> coins, no emperor can change it

</details>

<details class="block-wrap" open id="block-2">
<summary><span class="section-label"><span class="block-num">02</span> Meet Richard Cantillon</span></summary>
<div class="block-inner">

<div class="block-meta-tags">
<span>Narrative/Anecdotal</span>
<span>Ethos</span>
</div>

<details open>
<summary><span class="section-label">Thesis</span></summary>
<div class="block-thesis">
Three hundred years ago, an economist figured out exactly how new money transfers wealth from the last receivers to the first -- and he profited from his own insight while his debtors were destroyed.
</div>
</details>

<details open>
<summary><span class="section-label">Argument</span></summary>

1. Richard Cantillon (c.1680s-1734): Irish-French economist, author of *Essai sur la Nature du Commerce en General* -- "the cradle of political economy"
2. <span class="glitch-hover" data-text="Cantillon effects">Cantillon effects</span>: the real changes in resource allocation between the time of new money creation and full price adjustment
3. First receivers spend at pre-inflation prices; last receivers face already-inflated prices
4. Cantillon personally profited by understanding John Law's Mississippi Company inflationary scheme -- made a fortune while his debtors were ruined
5. He was pursued by lawsuits, criminal charges, and murder plots until his death
6. The effect is 300 years old -- named by a man who lived and died by it. Nothing has changed.

</details>

<details open>
<summary><span class="section-label">Citations</span></summary>
<ul class="citation-list">
<li>"When the government can print money, the people that benefit the most are the people that get access to that money first... rich people get richer without adding much" <span class="cite-source">-- Jimmy Song (#67)</span></li>
<li>"The true trickle down economics is the Cantillon effect" <span class="cite-source">-- Author (#9)</span></li>
</ul>
</details>

<details open>
<summary><span class="section-label">Evidence</span></summary>

- Cantillon's *Essai* published posthumously 1755
- Jevons called it the first complete treatise on economics
- Mississippi Company speculation: 1710s-1720s

</details>

<details open>
<summary><span class="section-label">Transition</span></summary>
<p class="block-transition">The <span class="glitch-hover" data-text="Cantillon Effect">Cantillon Effect</span> is not a theory discovered in a library -- it was lived, exploited, and named by a man who profited from it three centuries ago. The mechanism he identified is exactly how the modern Federal Reserve operates, which is what the next block breaks down step by step.</p>
</details>

<details open>
<summary><span class="section-label">Rhetorical Moves</span></summary>

- Story of a real man -- not abstract theory but a person who exploited the mechanism
- "Nothing has changed" -- bridges 300 years to present in one sentence
- "Cantillionaires" wordplay (save for later in the block or next block)

</details>

<details class="block-wrap" open id="block-3">
<summary><span class="section-label"><span class="block-num">03</span> How the Money Printer Actually Works</span></summary>
<div class="block-inner">

<div class="block-meta-tags">
<span>Causal / Logical</span>
<span>Logos</span>
</div>

<details open>
<summary><span class="section-label">Thesis</span></summary>
<div class="block-thesis">
The Federal Reserve doesn't hand money to regular people -- it gives it to banks, who invest it in financial markets, and by the time new money reaches the average person, prices have already risen.
</div>
</details>

<details open>
<summary><span class="section-label">Argument</span></summary>

1. How does the Fed print more dollars? Not by handing cash to citizens
2. Fed gives new money to banks via open market operations and QE
3. Banks invest or lend to other financial institutions, who invest it
4. Money trickles: financial community → stock market → venture capital → bonds → real estate
5. By the time it reaches the average person, it has exchanged hands several times
6. The ones who get money first benefit from new dollars; the people who get it last do not
7. This isn't a conspiracy theory -- it's the literal mechanics of monetary expansion
8. M2: $15.45T (Feb 2020) → $21.70T (Mar 2022) -- $6.25 trillion added in 2 years, ~40% increase
9. Three consequences: (1) price signal distortion, (2) increased business input costs, (3) asset <span class="glitch-hover" data-text="inflation">inflation</span>
10. New money enters via government → flows to connected companies first → false supply/demand signals → capital misallocation
11. Human capital misallocation: "smart people get sucked in closer to the government and not servicing the rest of the economy"
12. Government only has three options for a deficit: raise taxes, cut spending, or print. They always print because the others cost votes.

</details>
