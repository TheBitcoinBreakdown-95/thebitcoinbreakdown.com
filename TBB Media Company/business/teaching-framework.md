# Teaching Framework -- TBB Content Design Reference

> Extracted from pedagogical research in `2025-2035 (old)/How to Teach/`. Use this when designing episodes, courses, workshops, or any educational content. Not a rigid template -- a toolkit to draw from based on what the content needs.

---

## Core Approach: Constructivist-Cognitivist Hybrid

TBB's teaching style uses **visual and narrative inputs** (cognitivist) to support **mental schema formation** while learners build understanding through **application and connection** (constructivist). This means:

- Lead with stories, analogies, and visuals -- not definitions
- Let the learner construct understanding by connecting new info to what they already know
- Avoid rote memorization or "here are the facts" delivery
- Test understanding through application ("what would happen if..."), not recall

This is the opposite of textbook teaching. It's closer to how a great dinner conversation teaches you something -- through narrative, surprise, and personal relevance.

## The Three Phases

Every piece of educational content (episode, workshop, lesson) moves through three phases. Not every piece needs all three at full depth, but the sequence matters.

### Phase 1: Inspire (Open with curiosity, not content)

**Goal:** Create a gap the learner wants to close. They should feel "I need to know more" before you teach anything.

**10 Tactics (use 1-3 per piece):**

1. **Burning Question** -- Open with an intellectual gap. "If the dollar lost all value tomorrow, what would you use as money?" Don't answer it. Let it sit.

2. **Mystery / Surprise** -- Lead with something unexpected. "The US dollar has lost 97% of its purchasing power since 1913. Most people don't know that."

3. **Personal Relevance (ME -> WE -> WORLD)** -- Start with how it affects the listener personally, expand to community, then global. "Your savings account is losing purchasing power every day. So is everyone else's. Here's why that's by design."

4. **Autonomy / Choice** -- Give the learner agency. "You can keep trusting institutions, or you can verify for yourself. Here's how."

5. **Mission Framing** -- Connect to something bigger. "This isn't about money. It's about whether the next generation has financial freedom."

6. **Inquiry-First** -- Ask questions before giving answers. Socratic method. "Why do you think governments don't want a money supply they can't control?"

7. **Emotional Hooks** -- Stories with stakes. Real people, real consequences. Venezuela, Zimbabwe, Cyprus, Canada truckers.

8. **Reflective Questions** -- Existential, not factual. "What would you do if your bank account was frozen tomorrow?"

9. **Gamification / Challenge** -- "By the end of this episode, you'll understand something 95% of economists get wrong."

10. **Peer Teaching** -- Design content so the viewer wants to explain it to someone else. "After this, you'll be able to explain Bitcoin to your parents in 2 minutes."

**Curiosity Loop:** Ignite (question) -> Engage (explore) -> Reflect (what surprised you) -> Empower (now you can) -> Celebrate (you get it)

### Phase 2: Absorb (Encode, store, retrieve)

**Goal:** Make the knowledge stick. Not just understood in the moment -- retrievable weeks later.

**10 Tactics (use 3-5 per piece):**

1. **Spaced Repetition** -- Revisit key concepts across episodes, not just within one. Build callbacks. "Remember in Episode 3 when we talked about the halving? Here's why that matters now."

2. **Retrieval Practice** -- Prompt the listener to recall before revealing. "Before I tell you, try to remember: how many bitcoin will ever exist?" Quizzes, reflection prompts, "pause and think" moments.

3. **Elaboration** -- Ask "why" and "how" questions. Use analogies. "Bitcoin's supply cap is like if there were only 21 million grains of gold in the entire universe -- and everyone knew exactly how many were left."

4. **Dual Coding** -- Combine words with visuals. Slides, diagrams, drawings, stock footage. Two encoding channels are stronger than one. This is why slide-based YouTube works for complex topics.

5. **Concrete Examples** -- Abstract concepts need tangible grounding. Don't just say "inflation erodes purchasing power." Say "In 1970, a house cost $23,000. Today it's $400,000. Your grandparents' savings didn't grow -- the ruler shrank."

6. **Interleaving** -- Mix related topics rather than drilling one concept to death. In a "Why Bitcoin?" episode, weave together inflation, censorship, and privacy rather than treating them as isolated units.

7. **Variation** -- Present the same concept in different contexts. Self-custody for a college student vs. a retiree vs. a Venezuelan shopkeeper.

8. **Desirable Difficulty** -- Don't make it too easy. Productive struggle = better retention. "This next concept is going to challenge what you think you know about money."

9. **Emotion / Surprise** -- Emotional memories stick. The hyperinflation footage, the Mt. Gox story, the "imagine your bank freezing your account" scenario.

10. **Sleep / Consolidation** -- For courses and multi-part series, spread learning across days. The brain consolidates during sleep. Daily short episodes > weekly marathon sessions.

**Meta-strategies:**
- **ELI5 Rule:** If you can't explain it to a 5-year-old, you don't understand it well enough. Forces clarity and reveals gaps in your own understanding.
- **Socratic Interrogation:** Strategic questioning that forces elaboration. Don't tell -- ask, then build on the answer.

### Phase 3: Deliver (How you actually teach it)

**Toolkit:**
- **Stories > facts.** A story about a Venezuelan family using Bitcoin to escape hyperinflation teaches more than a chart.
- **Feynman Technique:** Explain simply, identify gaps, go back to source, explain again. If you can't make it simple, you haven't learned it.
- **Chunk and Zoom:** Start with the big picture (chunk), then zoom into details. "Bitcoin is digital money that no one controls. Now let me show you exactly how that works."
- **Think-Pair-Share (for workshops):** Present a question, let people think individually, discuss in pairs, share with group.
- **Concept Maps:** Visual diagrams showing how ideas connect. Great for "how Bitcoin works" explainers.
- **Analogies:** The backbone of accessible teaching. Bitcoin mining = lottery tickets, blockchain = public ledger at the town square, private keys = combination to your vault.
- **Exit Tickets (for workshops/courses):** "What's one thing that surprised you? What's one thing you're still confused about?"

## Applying to TBB Content Types

### Podcast Episodes
- **Inspire:** Open with a burning question or emotional hook (30-60 seconds)
- **Absorb:** Use elaboration, concrete examples, and analogies throughout
- **Deliver:** Oratory style -- dense, pre-written, conviction. Riff on the script, don't read it flat.
- Close with a reflective question or call to action ("Go explain this to someone you care about")

### YouTube Explainers
- **Inspire:** Visual hook -- hyperinflation footage, surprising stat, provocative question
- **Absorb:** Dual coding is the superpower here. Slides + narration = two encoding channels. Use diagrams, charts, visual metaphors.
- **Deliver:** Chunk and zoom. Big picture first, then detail. Keep slides clean -- one idea per slide.

### Workshops (In-Person)
- **Inspire:** Roleplay, group challenge, or burning question
- **Absorb:** Think-pair-share, hands-on wallet setup, peer teaching ("now explain it to your partner")
- **Deliver:** Interactive. Exit tickets. Follow-up resources.

### Courses (Multi-Part)
- **Inspire:** Mission framing at the start. Recurring callbacks to "why this matters."
- **Absorb:** Spaced repetition across lessons. Quizzes. Variation (same concept, different contexts).
- **Deliver:** Short daily lessons > long weekly lectures. Build in retrieval practice.

## Example Lesson Structure (from archive)

**"The Collapse of Trust: What is Money, Really?"**

1. **Ignite** -- Hyperinflation video montage (Zimbabwe, Venezuela, Depression)
2. **Explore** -- Burning question: "If everyone woke up and dollars had no value, what would happen?"
3. **Build Context** -- 10-12 min interactive lecture (barter -> fiat -> inflation -> Bitcoin teaser)
4. **Challenge** -- 2030 roleplay: "Rebuild trust in a new monetary system." Groups: Economists, Central Bankers, Startups, Business Owners, Journalists
5. **Reflect** -- Presentations + Socratic questioning
6. **Debrief** -- Exit journal: "What surprised you?" "What do you believe now?" "What are you still wondering?"
7. **Bonus** -- Bitcoin explainer homework + "Explain like I'm 5" essay

This lesson uses: burning question, emotional hook, personal relevance, autonomy (groups choose approach), inquiry-first, mission framing, peer teaching, and reflective questions. It's the gold standard for workshop design.

## Learning Theories (Reference)

For deeper design decisions, the six major learning theories and when each applies:

| Theory | Core Idea | Use When |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Behaviorism (Skinner) | Stimulus-response + reinforcement | Building habits (DCA reminders, security checklists) |
| Cognitivism (Bruner) | Mental processes, schema building | Explaining how Bitcoin works (diagrams, mental models) |
| Constructivism (Piaget) | Learners build knowledge through active engagement | Workshops, courses, hands-on activities |
| Social Learning (Bandura) | Modeling, observation, social interaction | JC Bitcoin meetups, community events, peer teaching |
| Humanism (Rogers) | Personal growth, autonomy, purpose, safety | Motivation-focused content ("why this matters to YOU") |
| Connectivism (Siemens) | Learning via networks + pattern recognition | Curated resource lists, connecting ideas across domains |

TBB's primary mode is **constructivist-cognitivist** (stories + visuals + application), with **humanist** elements (autonomy, purpose) driving the inspiration layer.
