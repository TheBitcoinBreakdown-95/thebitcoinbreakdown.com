## Slide 13: HISTORY MOMENT -- The Cypherpunk Manifesto (1993)

- Written by Eric Hughes, March 9, 1993
- "Privacy is necessary for an open society in the electronic age"
- Cypherpunks write code -- they don't ask for permission
- Direct lineage: Cypherpunks -> Bitcoin -> Nostr -> White Noise

**Image:** Screenshot of the Cypherpunk Manifesto text or 90s-era crypto punk aesthetic

**Speaker Notes:**
On March 9, 1993 -- almost exactly 33 years ago -- Eric Hughes published the Cypherpunk Manifesto. "Privacy is necessary for an open society in the electronic age." This wasn't just philosophy -- cypherpunks wrote code. PGP encryption, anonymous remailers, digital cash experiments. That movement led directly to Bitcoin, which led to Nostr, which led to White Noise -- the tool we're about to demo. The manifesto's core argument: privacy isn't secrecy. A private matter is something you don't want the whole world to know. A secret is something you don't want anybody to know. Privacy is the power to selectively reveal yourself. That's exactly what White Noise does.

- Private encrypted messenger built on Nostr
- No phone number, no email, no central server
- Same encryption as Signal (MLS protocol) but decentralized
- "The chat with no phone number and no central server to shut down"

**Image:** White Noise app screenshot or logo

**Speaker Notes:**
White Noise is a private encrypted messenger built on Nostr. No phone number, no email, no central server. It uses MLS protocol -- the same encryption standard as Signal -- but runs on Nostr relays instead of Signal's central servers. Why does this matter? Apple just removed end-to-end encryption in the UK under government pressure. The EU's Chat Control legislation has 679 scientists warning against mass surveillance. Jeff Gardner, one of the builders, said it straight: "These tools might make the difference between continued democracy and a descent into a global authoritarian surveillance regime." It's built by Max Hillebrand (former CEO of zkSNACKs/Wasabi Wallet) and funded by OpenSats and the Human Rights Foundation. Currently in alpha -- available on TestFlight, Zapstore, and Android APK. We're going to demo it live.

## Slide 15: TECH -- Bark/Ark

- New Bitcoin Layer 2: skip Lightning channel complexity, stay self-custodial
- Hundreds of users share one on-chain UTXO via a tree of pre-signed transactions
- You hold a Virtual UTXO (VTXO) -- can exit to on-chain anytime
- Not replacing Lightning -- complementing it. Ark is the on-ramp.

**Image:** Ark protocol diagram or side-by-side Lightning vs Ark comparison graphic

**Speaker Notes:**
Lightning is powerful but onboarding is painful. Opening a channel means an on-chain transaction, managing liquidity, dealing with routing failures. Most people give up and use custodial wallets. Ark is a new Layer 2 invented by Burak Keceli in 2023. The core idea: hundreds of users share a single on-chain UTXO through a tree of pre-signed off-chain transactions. Each user holds a Virtual UTXO that can be broadcast on-chain anytime to retrieve your bitcoin. Bark is the Rust implementation built by Second (Steven Roose, ex-Blockstream). Ark Labs just raised $5.2M from Tether for stablecoin integration. Key tradeoff: VTXOs expire after ~30 days and need refreshing. But you get no channel setup, no liquidity management, and you can pay any Lightning invoice directly from your Ark balance. It's not a replacement for Lightning -- it's the on-ramp that makes Lightning accessible.
