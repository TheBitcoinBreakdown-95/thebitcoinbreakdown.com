# Your First Project

That hello file you created in the last lesson? It proved everything works. But it wasn't real work. You don't need an AI agent to write you a greeting.

So now we're going to do something that actually matters to you.

---

## Pick Something Real

This is the single most important instruction in the entire course. Do not skip it. Do not pick a hypothetical exercise. Do not do the AI equivalent of "Hello World."

Pick something you actually need to get done.

Not something you might need someday. Something that's been sitting in the back of your mind... a plan you haven't made, a letter you haven't written, a project you haven't organized, research you've been putting off.

Here are some examples to get your brain going:

- A business plan for a side project you've been thinking about
- A workout or meal plan you've been meaning to make
- A research document about a topic you need to understand for work
- A lesson plan if you teach anything
- A budget for an upcoming trip or event
- An organized set of notes from a class or workshop
- A cover letter for a job application
- A comparison of options you're deciding between (apartments, cars, software tools, schools)
- A guide you wish existed for something you know about

Pick one. Something specific. Something where you'll actually use the output.

The reason this matters: you're about to see AI create real work product in real time. If the project is fake, you won't feel the difference. If it's real, you will.

---

## Set Up the Folder

Open VS Code. Create a new folder for your project, or use the one you made in the last lesson.

The folder name should describe what's in it. "Business Plan" or "Trip to Portugal" or "Home Renovation Research" or whatever your project is. Keep it simple. You're going to be looking at this name in your sidebar for a while.

Open that folder in VS Code (File > Open Folder).

---

## Start Talking

Here's where it gets fun.

Open the Claude Code panel and describe your project. Not in keywords. Not in bullet points. Talk to it the way you'd talk to a smart assistant who just showed up on their first day.

Something like:

> "I'm planning a two-week trip to Portugal in September for two people. We want to split time between Lisbon and Porto, with maybe a day trip to Sintra. Budget is around $5,000 total not including flights. Can you create a rough itinerary, a packing list, and a budget breakdown? Put each one in its own file."

Or:

> "I run a small landscaping business and I've been meaning to write up a proper business plan. I have about 15 regular clients, two employees, and I want to expand into commercial properties next year. Can you create a starting outline for a business plan and put it in a file? Don't make it generic -- use the details I just gave you."

Or:

> "I need to understand how property taxes work in New Jersey. I just bought my first house and I have no idea what to expect. Can you research this and create a plain-English guide that covers what I'll owe, when it's due, how assessments work, and what I can do if I think the assessment is wrong? Put it in a document."

Notice what's happening. You're not writing a "prompt." You're not trying to hack the AI with magic words. You're just... explaining what you need. The same way you'd explain it to a person.

---

## Watch the Files Appear

After you hit enter, watch the sidebar.

Files start appearing. The agent tells you what it's doing as it works. And when it's done, you've got documents on your computer that didn't exist sixty seconds ago.

Click on one. Read it.

Some of it will be exactly what you wanted. Some of it won't be quite right. Some of it might surprise you with stuff you didn't think to ask for. That's all fine. This is a first draft, not a final product.

Here's the thing that trips people up: you're not done. This isn't vibing. You don't just accept whatever came out and move on.

---

## Direct the Revision

Read through what the agent created. Find something that's off. Maybe the budget is missing a category. Maybe the itinerary has too many museums and not enough restaurants. Maybe the business plan outline is too generic.

Now tell the agent what to change. Be specific.

> "The budget is missing food costs. Add a section for meals, assuming we eat out twice a day at mid-range restaurants."

> "The business plan outline is too generic. In the market analysis section, focus specifically on commercial landscaping contracts in central New Jersey, not residential."

> "This packing list has way too many clothes. We're packing carry-on only. Revise for a minimalist approach."

The agent revises the file. Not in a new chat message... it updates the actual file on your computer. You can see the changes right there in VS Code.

This is the workflow. You direct. The agent executes. You review. You redirect. The files get better with each pass.

And here's what's different from vibing: every revision builds on what's already there. The agent reads the files it already created. It doesn't start from scratch. It doesn't forget what you told it five minutes ago. The project accumulates.

---

## The Moment It Clicks

At some point during this process, something is going to hit you.

You're going to look at the files on your computer... files that are useful, organized, and specific to your actual life... and you're going to realize that ten minutes ago they didn't exist. You described what you wanted in plain English, and a workspace full of real documents appeared.

Not answers in a chat log. Not suggestions in a browser tab. Real files. Your files.

This is not a chatbot. This is a workspace. And you just used it for the first time.

---

## Before You Move On

Take a minute. Look at what you created. Open each file. Read through it. Make one more revision, something specific that would make one of the files more useful to you.

You've got a project folder with real content in it now. In the next lesson, you're going to teach your Agent Control Center who you are, so every future conversation starts smarter than this one did.
